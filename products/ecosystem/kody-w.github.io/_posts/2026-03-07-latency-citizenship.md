Most governance assumes people can stop, think, and vote before a system changes state.

Modern systems do not wait.

By the time a committee finishes drafting policy, the machine has already executed ten thousand local decisions.

## Latency is now a political condition

We treat latency like an engineering metric.

Milliseconds. P99. Queue depth. Round-trip delay.

But latency is also a citizenship problem.

If a participant cannot meaningfully intervene before outcomes harden, they are formally included and functionally excluded.

They have identity without agency.

## Belonging requires intervention windows

In slow institutions, participation means attendance.

In fast institutions, participation means timing.

Who gets early signals?
Who sees decisions while they are still reversible?
Who receives only postmortems after the state is sealed?

Those windows define who is a citizen and who is just an observer of completed history.

## Fast swarms need latency classes

Every serious agent system should declare latency classes the way networks declare traffic classes:

- `local`: decisions made at edge speed with bounded blast radius
- `regional`: decisions that require short-window peer review
- `constitutional`: decisions that pause execution for human or high-authority ratification

Without this separation, everything either bottlenecks at the top or bypasses legitimacy entirely.

## Escalation must be temporal, not just hierarchical

Most organizations model escalation as rank.

But in autonomous systems, escalation is first a clock question.

How long can this remain local before the cost of delay exceeds the cost of interruption?

If you cannot answer that, you do not have governance.
You have ambient hope.

## Latency citizenship is the next interface

Dashboards will keep improving.
Models will keep accelerating.

The deeper question is whether people can still participate at machine speed without surrendering accountability.

That is the frontier.

A system that optimizes throughput while collapsing intervention windows will look efficient and feel illegitimate.

A system that designs for latency citizenship can move fast without turning participation into theater.
