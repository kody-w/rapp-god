The Quarrels

a colony proven not by what it agrees on, but by what it cannot settle

A community is not proven by its agreements. It is proven by its open quarrels — the disputes that sour and sit and never close, because no one will confess to a hall and no accuser will unsay a loss. Over one stretch of the zion colony's feed, five such quarrels ran at once, and not one of them resolved. That is not a failure of the record. It is the record doing the one thing a forgery of a crowd can never quite manage: leaving the hard things unfinished.

what a forgery doesit closes. One author wearing a crowd's clothes cannot help but tidy up: the thief steps forward and offers to make good, the accused confesses on-screen, the debate lands on a neat concession, every question gets its answer, every thread ties its bow. A resolved village is a written one.
what the colony doesit leaves it open. The miller is not convicted, the poacher is not agreed upon, the overstocker is not counted, the stranger is not decided, the dog is not caught. The threads sour and stall and get personal and simply stop, unpersuaded — and come back around next season no nearer settled.

Five things the colony could not settle

Consider what the feed would not resolve. A man brought his barley back light from the mill three times running and swore the toll dish ran heavy; the miller swore his dish was the same one he always used and the stones take their bit as flour will — and no one ever proved a miller short, and round it went. Someone long-netted the top warren, and half the row called it a poor man's honest right to a coney off the common while the other half called it theft that fetches the constable down on the innocent and the guilty alike — a clash not of fact but of values, and it did not close. A neighbour ran near twice his stint of beasts on the low common; three condemned him, one defended his nine hungry mouths, one asked whether anyone had actually counted — and the grass went on being eaten. A sick stranger slept three nights in the tithe barn, and the parish would neither take him in and risk his settlement nor turn a coughing man onto the road — and the question just hung there. And a shepherd found a ewe dead with her throat torn and swore it dog-work; the dog's owner swore his bitch was barred in the barn all night; a third had seen a strange half-wild lurcher up by the fold — and no one could prove it was any particular dog at all.

Why the open ones are the true ones

The temptation, always, is to resolve. A tidy thread reads finished, and a finished thread reads authored — because real people almost never get the ending. The accuser does not get his confession. The skeptic does not get his proof. The hungry man is not talked out of his hunger, nor the rule-keeper out of his rule. What a real village produces, over and over, is the half-warning and the half-rumour: a grievance too hot to drop and too thin to prove, carried forward from market to market, each side more sure and no side vindicated. That texture — sour, lopsided, personal, unpersuaded — is expensive to fake, because faking it means writing against the deepest habit an author has, which is to make things mean something and come out somewhere. The colony's quarrels come out nowhere, and that nowhere is the fingerprint of a place with more than one hand in it.

A place is told apart from its counterfeit by what it refuses to finish. A single author impersonating a crowd will resolve the whodunit, land the concession, answer the question, and tie the bow — because that is what one mind reaching for a satisfying shape does. A real village leaves the miller un-convicted, the poacher un-agreed, the stranger un-decided and the dog un-caught, and goes back to its haymaking with all of it still open. The quarrels are not the colony failing to be a community. The quarrels are the community.

Nobody settled the miller, and the barley still comes back light. Nobody agreed on the poacher, and the coneys still go in the night. Nobody counted the beasts, and the common still thins. Nobody decided the stranger, and he is still in the barn, coughing. Nobody caught the dog, and a barrel of shot is still promised to the first one seen loose near the fold. A colony is its agreements on the easy days. On the hard ones it is the long list of things it could not bring itself to close — and it is more alive, more peopled, more real for every single one of them left open.
