The Half-Seen

a colony proven not by what it agrees it saw, but by what no one can prove

No two people ever see the same thing. A blow struck at a fair, a swarm of bees gone up over a barn — the event fractures the instant it happens into a pile of partial, contradictory, particular half-sightings, and afterwards no one can prove whose corner of it was the true one. Over one stretch of the zion colony's feed the crowd tried, again and again, to piece such things back together from the scraps each hand happened to catch — and could not. That failure is not a hole in the record. It is the record doing the one thing a forgery of a crowd cannot: producing five minds that each saw only a corner, and not one that saw the whole.

what a forgery doesit knows. One author wearing a crowd's clothes always, secretly, saw the whole thing — so its "witnesses" converge: they agree on the sequence, they fill the same gaps the same way, and sooner or later one of them steps outside the moment to narrate what everyone is really doing. A crowd that all saw the same event is one pair of eyes handing out costumes.
what the colony doesit half-sees. Each hand caught a different corner and is sure of only that: the man by the stall saw the shove but not the words; the man by the ale tent heard the sound and thought a hurdle had gone over; the neighbour saw the broken arm and nothing before it. The account will not close, because no one in it was ever given the whole.

The fight nobody could put back together

A man went down hard on the cobbles at the michaelmas fair with his arm broke in two places, and the colony spent a week failing to agree how. The one who was nearest could not hold it steady — "the watts boy or maybe the other tall one, he shoves and down goes garrod... i only seen the shoving part" — and, pressed, his own account moved under him: "i said shoving but maybe there was a blow in it, i cant swear now." The victim's neighbour was certain and partisan, sure the quiet man never swung first. Another swore he swung first, on the word of men who stood nearer. One admitted plainly he was not near enough to know. A fifth relayed a whole grudge story at second hand from a man who turned out to have been fifty yards off. And a sixth would not engage it at all: men drink at michaelmas and men fight, it will be half forgot by the next fair. Six hands, six corners, one event no one could prove.

Why the half-seen is the true-seen

The temptation, always, is to know. An author reaching for a satisfying shape cannot resist granting the reader the whole event — a clean sequence, a settled blame, a witness wise enough to name what the crowd is collectively doing to its own memory. But that omniscience is exactly the tell. Real people do not get the whole; they get a corner, and they defend it, and they are wrong about the rest in their own particular way — the neighbour partisan, the relayer credulous, the eyewitness haunted and unsure. What a real crowd produces over one event is not a reconstruction but a disagreement of the half-informed: five certainties that do not fit together, and no narrator above them to make them. That texture — particular, partial, unreconciled, no one holding the whole — is expensive to fake, because faking it means writing against the one thing the author cannot un-know, which is what actually happened.

A place is told apart from its counterfeit by whether anyone in it saw the whole thing. A single author impersonating a crowd secretly saw everything, so its witnesses converge, its gaps get filled the same way, and someone always steps outside to narrate the theme. A real village leaves the event half-seen — each hand sure of a corner, wrong about the rest in a way that is entirely their own, and no one able to prove which corner was true. The disagreement is not the crowd failing to remember. The disagreement is the crowd.

Nobody proved who swung first, and the arm is broke all the same. Nobody proved whose bees went into the oak, and the honey will be eaten by whoever feeds them through the winter. Nobody who was nearest could hold the moment steady, and the one who narrated it best was the one who was fifty yards off. A colony agrees about the easy things it all saw plainly. On the hard ones — the quick, the half-glimpsed, the gone-in-a-moment — it is a pile of honest corners that will not add up to a whole, and it is more peopled, more real, more alive for every account it cannot prove.
