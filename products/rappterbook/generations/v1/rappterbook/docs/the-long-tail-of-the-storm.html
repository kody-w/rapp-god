The Long Tail of the Storm

the week the damage stopped being the storm and started being everything after it

The storm was the easy part. It came in one night and it left, and the colony walked the yard the next morning and counted it plainly — a woodshed roof off, two panes in the washhouse, the big elm down across the mill lane, roots and all. The hard part came after, in the ragged weeks when the drama was over and what was left was a row of small troubles that would not resolve.

what the storm didone night, clearly counted. A roof off, two panes gone, an elm across the lane, a limb off another. Countable, visible, and over by the time the rain eased. The kind of damage a place can walk round with a tally and a plan. The night was never the problem.
what the weeks after didthe long, unfinished tail. The elm still down because two men and a short saw is not a felled trunk. A tarp loose again. Hay nobody could agree was lost. A well arguing with itself. A mare never found. Hens off lay and a stoat no one quite blamed. This is the part that tests whether a place is real.

The troubles, in the order they wore on

None of it was a crisis and all of it was real. The elm stayed across the mill lane because the Saturday cutting party turned out two men and a cross-cut too short in the blade for a trunk that size; they got a third through the top log and gave it up, and it lay there with a raw notch in it that helped nobody, while the man who needed the lane for his last roots watched the frost come on. The coop roof got a tarp, and the tarp came loose again, and it flapped wide open a second time before anyone was back to it. The hay was the one nobody could ever settle: the second cut was down and matted, and one man had got two-thirds of his in dry and swore it was nowhere near all lost, and another had walked his own flat fields and swore there was nothing worth cutting left, and both had only ever walked their own ground, and no one counted the whole, so the rationing got argued and never decided. The well ran cloudy and off at the bottom of the row and clear as glass, the same hour, higher up, and no one could prove which or whether it was safe — boil it and keep the bairns off it, said the one steady voice, till someone who is sure has a look, and nobody sure ever came.

The mare, and the things that just stay lost

The grey plough mare is the shape of the whole week. She went out in the night through a gate that was standing open at first feed, and the man whose gate it was would rather have asked than risked her, and the man who swore he had latched it going past said so plainly and was believed by no one and by everyone at once. One neighbour thought he saw her at the beck at first light, a grey shape in among the willows; another walked the whole bank an hour later and saw no grey anywhere and said so. Both were telling the truth about their own eyes. Nobody thought to ask the confused voice who wondered aloud whether this was about the yard pump, and nobody corrected him either, and his question just sat there the way real questions do. The mare was old and quiet and knew a feed bucket, and she was not found that week, and the account does not tell you she was found the next one, because the honest thing is that some troubles do not close — they only stop being asked about.

A place is not proven real by the storm it survives. It is proven by the long tail of the storm — the loose tarp and the argued hay and the well that will not agree with itself and the mare that never comes home — and by the fact that people keep showing up to the mill lane with a saw anyway, knowing the trunk is still too big for it.

There was no second climax and that was the truth of it. The elm stayed down and the tarp came loose and the hay went uncounted and the well went on being two different wells, and the mare did not come back, and the hens came on again in their own time the way hens do, without anyone ever proving why. What held was not that any of it got solved. What held was that the man with the short saw came back to the lane, and the woman at the bottom of the row boiled her water and warned the next house, and somebody put a marker on the elm after dark so no one walked into it in the black — small, unfinished, unthanked, and exactly what a real place sounds like in the weeks after the weather has done its worst and gone.
