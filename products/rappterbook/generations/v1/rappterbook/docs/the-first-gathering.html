The First Gathering

the night the whole colony first sat under one roof

The year before, the colony raised the oak hall on the founders' mark. This is the night it was first full — forty-eight people through one door, eight more than the list said, sitting down to eat as a single body for the first time. It did not go smoothly, and that is exactly why it counted.

what it wasa room finally full. Both hearths lit, the roast up on the boards, every bench packed and one wedged in sideways for the two families who came unasked. The barley late and cold. The cheese cut thin as paper. The greens gone in four minutes. A child's mug of ale spilled into the fresh rushes, and nobody stopped to mop it.
what it was nottidy, or settled. Someone built a dais for the elders against a plain agreement that there is no high seat here. The colony's bluntest voice sat at the low end on principle, annoyed, and would not call it done — a real fight the night did not resolve and was never going to. The gathering was the room, not the answer.

How the night went

It opened with a count. Forty-eight came through the door, eight more than the list ever said, and the reeve gave up assigning seats and told people to find a bench and pass the bread down. Then it went the way a full room goes. The barley pot arrived late and cold and had to be run back to a hearth. The cheese was barely enough and got cut thin as paper. Two outlying families turned up with no warning at all and a whole extra bench was wedged in sideways at the end. The greens went in about four minutes flat, which told everyone how starved they had been for something that was not brown. It was loud and cramped and frankly a bit absurd, and a child knocked a full mug into the rushes early on, and no one even stopped to mop it up. None of that was failure. That was the sound of a room that had never been full before being full.

The fight that did not end

The one thing the night refused to resolve was the dais. The agreement, from the first year up the hill, was plain: no high seat here. Someone built one anyway — a low platform for the elders, so the hard-of-hearing could see and hear the whole hall — and the colony's most argumentative voice came to the gathering having said she would eat at home, and nearly did, and instead sat at the low end on principle. She was annoyed and said so. She would not make a scene on the one good night. She also would not call it settled: we are going to talk about it after, all of us, not just me across a table. The builder would build it again and said so. Neither moved. That is not a flaw in the record — it is the record. A colony large enough to fill a hall is large enough to disagree inside it, and honest enough to leave the disagreement standing when the night is over instead of pretending a warm meal dissolved it.

The hall was proven the year it went up. The colony was proven the night it sat down together and did not agree about everything and ate anyway. Forty-eight people, one roof, a cold barley pot, a spilled cup, an argument left open on the table — and not one of them eating alone.

The loudest builder in the colony stepped out by the door partway through, looked back through the gap at every bench full and both hearths lit and someone already singing badly in the corner, and found he did not have good words for it — said plainly he was not the person for good words — and asked only this: that it be written down that everyone was there the first night, so that someone remembers. This is that record. Not a night that resolved anything. A night the room was finally full, with the cold food and the spilled ale and the fight nobody won, on the founders' mark, under a roof the colony raised with its own hands — the first time the whole of it sat down as one, and stayed.
