The Hall Raised

the year the colony built the first thing that was only its own

For two years the colony lived inside a shell someone else half-built and left. This is the year it raised the first structure that is entirely its own — the oak hall — on the founders' mark, by hands that had been strangers a winter before, and it did not go up easily.

what it tooka whole third year. A thaw waited for rather than wished for. Oak hauled down an ice ridge and four beams short. A wall lost in a night to a cold snap and rebuilt. Fuel that was hoarded all winter, burned to tent the fresh courses warm. Two crews on rotation so nobody's back gave out, and a pace held on purpose so nobody fell.
what it meansone room, all of us. Not a thing built to survive a winter — the first thing built for after. One room big enough to hold the whole colony at once to argue in, on the exact stone the founders marked as the origin, with the record of everyone it cost to get here hung on a shelf by the door.

How it went up

It started with a refusal. When someone called an early thaw on a feeling, the colony would not move the schedule until the gauges confirmed it — and they did, three days early on the data, not the hunch. The first course went down on the level bench, squared to the boundary stone the survey was drawn from, and forty people came unasked to watch a single stone set and started clapping when it landed true. Then the year did what years do. The oak came four beams short and the hall was shortened by a bay rather than bled for. A cold snap caught the green mortar and slumped the top of the north wall a hand's width out; nobody was under it, and nobody broke. The colony salvaged the sound stone below the slump, tented the fresh courses warm over braziers, checked the real festival buffer — nineteen days, not the rumored month — and held its safe pace on purpose. Course by course, warmed this time, the walls climbed to meet a door frame cut from the very beam that had cracked in the haul.

The wall that fell, and the line that held

The night the wall came down was the test, and not of the mortar. It is easy to keep going when the clapping is fresh; it is harder the morning after a gut punch. What steadied the colony was not a speech. It was one plain line someone finally understood in the founders' brief — lost the east wall to a frost, rebuilt — about the founders' own first hall. They lost a wall too. They rebuilt too. It is a small thing to inherit, the memory that the people before you also failed a wall and kept going, but on a cold morning with two courses in the mud it was the whole thing. The most argumentative voice in the colony had already stood at the back of the first course and cried; now the whole place simply picked the stone back up. A hall is not proven by going up clean. It is proven by going up again.

Two years ago the colony sheltered in a stranger's ruin and counted the days until spring. This year it built its own place to stand and stopped counting. That is the distance travelled — from surviving inside what the founders left, to raising, on their exact mark and steadied by their admitted failures, the first room this colony can call only its own.

The oak hall stands shorter by a bay than it was drawn, with four windows it can shutter against the cold and a door hung from a beam that cracked and was saved anyway. It went up on the founders' origin stone, by the hands of people who arrived here strangers, and it fell once and was raised again. On a shelf by its door sit the two logs — the cold record of what happened and the cost-log of what it cost, Marek and Senna and the rest — so that everyone who ever sits in that one warm room, arguing, will know exactly whose winter bought it. The colony no longer lives in what it was handed. It lives in what it built, and it built it the hard way, on purpose, together, and only once it was sure the ground would hold.
