The Ordinary Days

the season the colony stopped having events and started having days

After the first gathering, and after the dais that went up against a plain agreement and came down in the night, the drama simply ran out. What was left was the harder test of whether a place is real — not the nights it sits whole under one roof, but the ordinary mornings after, when nothing is dramatic and nothing quite resolves.

what the days heldsmall, unfinished things. A fox under the loose fence and three hens gone. A perimeter that was everyone's job and so nobody's. A well that runs cloudy for one person and clear for another. A hearth that smokes on green wood. Two paths to the mill, each sworn faster. And a run of rules and rotas nobody ever wrote down, found missing one at a time.
what it proveda place, not a stage. That the colony holds together not because its arguments end well but because people show up to the next fence anyway. Most of these threads never resolved. One did, on a Tuesday, over which way to notch a stone. That ratio — almost nothing settled, the odd small thing conceded — is what a lived-in place actually sounds like.

The frictions, in the order they came

None of it was a crisis and all of it was real. The fox came under the loose wire at the compost corner that one voice had flagged for a month and nobody had mended, because the fence was a shared job and a shared job is no one's; three hens and the grey chick were gone by morning. The corner got mended, and the byre stretch, and then the arguing started about which had been the harder work. The well ran cloudy after the rain, the way it always has for the person who has drawn it eight years — and clear as glass, the same hour, for the person who draws it every dawn, and neither could move the other because both were only ever describing their own bucket. The hearth smoked when someone burned green wood, and every fix — sit further back, open the far shutter, trim the wick — was refused, because the shutter was painted shut and the point was the wet wood. And the paths to the mill: upper or lower, each defended by someone who had timed it and was certain, unwinnable because they were both right, about their own legs, under their own load.

The things nobody wrote down

The quiet thread under all of it was a record that kept turning out not to exist. Was the fence a rota with names on it, or just whoever notices — nobody could find the list, and one person was fairly sure there had been one the first year and no idea where it went. Was the no-high-seat rule ever actually written in the founding record — someone went and looked, through the record and the boundary agreement both, and it was in neither. Which way does the tally stone get notched on a fresh face — genuinely nobody could remember what was decided. A colony discovers, one small missing page at a time, how much of what holds it together was never set down at all, only remembered, and remembered differently by each person who was there. That is not a failure. It is just what a place is before it becomes an archive: a set of agreements carried in a dozen heads that do not quite match.

A place is not proven real by the nights it sits whole under one roof. It is proven by the mornings after — when the hens are dead and the well is arguing with itself and the dais is still on the floor because moving it is nobody's job — and people show up, and bicker, and mend the fence anyway.

There was no climax to this stretch and that was the whole of it. The fox got in and the fences got mended and the well went on being two different wells to two different people, and the smoke still gathered when the wood came in wet, and half the colony walked up to the mill and half walked down and both of them got there. Somebody, on an ordinary afternoon, over the one small question of which end of a stone is the older count, said fair enough, you are right, and cut it top to bottom — and that single conceded notch, in a season where almost nothing else was, is exactly how you know it was real people and not a story. The dramas end. The days do not. The colony lives in the days.
