The Cost-Log

the year the colony learned to write down what it cost · not just what it counted

For two years the colony kept exactly one log: the cold record, the counts and the dates, exact and safe and completely silent about the dead. This is the year it started the second one — and found that the way to begin was not to write the fear, but to write a name.

the record we always keptwhat happened. Stores counted twice, sols dated, the pump logged, the sky logged, every crock and cord written down in a hand that never shook. It kept the colony alive and it never once said what the keeping cost. A ledger can hold a whole winter and not a single grief.
the log we could not startwhat it cost. Agreed to in principle and then left blank for a week, because the first line meant putting your own fear on a page a stranger reads in fifty years. The record was never the hard part. The remembering was, and it sat there empty, daring someone to go first.

How the first line got written

The two-logs plan came out of an argument the colony could not win: the founding had drifted. The counts from that first winter no longer matched the story everyone told about it — the memory had gone heavy where the record stayed thin, and no one could say which one they were allowed to keep. The answer, when it came, was to stop choosing: keep the cold record for what happened, and start a cost-log for what it cost, and let the two stand side by side. Then the cost-log sat blank for a week, because a feeling is an impossible thing to be first to write. So a welcomer stopped trying. Instead of the fear, they wrote a name: Sol forty-one, Marek, lost to the cold in the night, and we did not have time to stop and feel it. Not grand. Just one true name and the memory there had never been time for — and far easier to write than one true feeling. By the next morning there was a second name under it, added by a different hand before it could lose its nerve.

the cost-log — the first entries, in the order they were finally written
• sol 41 · Marek · the cold, in the night · no time to stop and feel it
• sol 9 · Senna · the first crossing · she got the last of them over the ridge and did not follow
• sol 63 · old Bexley · the fever · it took the tired ones first and he had carried the most
• sol 77 · the child with no name yet · born too early into too hard a winter
• … and the names still being added, one steady hand at a time, whenever someone can

Why a name, and not the fear

The insight is small and it holds the whole thing up: a feeling flattens, and a name does not. Try to write the grief of that first winter and you get a paragraph that is true for no one in particular; write a name and a sol and one plain cause, and you get something true enough that the next person can add theirs beside it without flinching. The cost-log stopped being a monument the moment it stopped trying to be one — it became a place the colony sets things down, the way the record is a place it sets numbers down. Nobody has to carry all of it. Everybody carries one line. That, it turns out, is how you hold your dead without being crushed under them: not a wound kept open and not a silence kept polite, but a page, and a hand, and one true name at a time.

Two years ago the colony counted what it had. This year it counted who it lost. That is the whole distance travelled — from a place that could only afford to record what kept it alive, to one safe enough, at last, to write down what living here cost, and to keep that record as carefully as the other one.

There are two logs now, and they sit side by side where the next colony will find them. One says what happened: the stores, the dates, the pump that held and the well that came in slow and real. The other says what it cost: Marek and Senna and old Bexley and the child with no name, and whoever is added tonight by whoever can bear to lift the pen. The colony spent two years believing the record was the hard part. It was not. The remembering was — and the day it learned to remember one true name at a time was the day it stopped only surviving its own history and started, quietly, keeping it.
