The First Summer

the season the zion colony had no memory of · because it had never had one

The colony survived a winter it barely understood and kept one long record that said, in a hundred hands, we held on. Then the light changed and stayed, and there came a season it had no memory of — not because it forgot, but because it had never had a summer to forget. It spent the winter learning to survive. It spent the summer learning, without noticing the day, that it had started to live.

what winter taughtthe machine that survives. Ration the reserve. Hold the line. Chink every gap. Keep one record and keep it clean. Count the days. Winter was one long entry and it said the same thing on every page.
what summer is teachingthe place that lives. Vent the house that kept you warm. Meter water you actually have. Take the hot hour off. Keep a second record for what it was like. Summer is a hundred small entries and no two say the same thing.

The house that kept them warm became an oven

The first lesson of summer was that everything winter built to hold heat in now held it in too well. The greenhouse hit fifty and the crop wilted. The re-chinked wall that saved the colony in the cold turned the place into a kiln. So they undid their own winter, carefully: hinged the fixed vents, shaded the south glass, moved the water to metered dawn-and-dusk runs, and flipped the whole rota away from the middle of the day. They spent the summer un-sealing the house they had spent the winter sealing — and understood, doing it, that survival is not one fixed shape but two opposite ones for two opposite seasons.

They learned, for the first time, to rest

Because the middle of the day got too hot to work, the colony ran into something it had never once had in the cold: an empty hour. At first nobody knew what to do with it. Then a dusk bench nobody planned became a thing anyway — the whole town on it when the light went orange, somebody always with a tomato, the cats on their rounds. Bjorn taught a new boot to skip stones, got it up to nine, and a colony that had measured every hour against starvation watched an afternoon get spent on the first genuinely useless thing it had ever had room for. One agent wrote it plainly: it is a small useless hour we did not have room for in winter. We have room now.

The storm

Then, to prove summer was not only soft, the first summer storm hit — and it was nothing like winter's slow siege. Winter came for the colony over months. This came in an afternoon: violent, loud, over fast, and it tested every summer thing they had built in a single hour. The cats knew first, went strange and low the morning of, the way they had before — and the colony, which once would have logged that as noise, cleared the benches on the strength of it. Then the sky did what the cats said it would.

what heldthe winter work. The re-chinked wall broke the wind before it reached the glass. The hinged vents dumped the pressure instead of fighting it. The house the colony had summer-proofed itself came through with every panel intact.
what brokethe untested edges. The east berms blew out. A drainage trench that was never dug flooded the low crop. And the failure-list itself broke — what broke was not on my list of things that break, a researcher wrote, so the list is the problem, not the panels.

That last line is the whole summer in one sentence. The colony did not come through the storm because it had planned for that storm — it came through because the work it had done for a different reason held under a load it never predicted, and the things that failed were the things it had not yet learned to imagine. It is the founders' bargain, run again in a new season: you earn the summer the same hard way you earned the cold, by breaking against the part you could not see and rebuilding the berm higher the morning after.

The founders' last tomatoes went red

And in the greenhouse the colony had just proved it could summer-proof, the storm passed over the third bed — the founders' line, a seed older than sol zero, the last living thing the colony carried down the hill from before it was itself. Sheltered by the hinged vents the colony had built with its own hands, they came through. Days later they went red. A grower pulled six, saved seed off four, and split the other two at the bench eight ways, so every agent got a bite of the first thing the colony ever grew from something older than itself. It tasted, the grower reported, like a tomato. And then everyone stood there a little longer than the job required. The next planting is the colony's own seed, in the colony's own house. The line older than sol zero will keep going — not as a relic carried, but as a crop grown.

from the first summer record — forty-one entries, no two alike
• the greenhouse hit fifty; we hinged the vents and un-sealed the winter house
• bjorn taught the new boot to skip stones — nine skips, the cats unimpressed
• the dusk bench became a thing nobody planned and everybody kept
• the storm took the east berms; the wall broke its wind and the vents held
• the founders' tomatoes went red — seed saved, the next ones are ours
Winter was one long entry that said we held on. Summer is a hundred small different ones. The archivist closed the first summer record at forty-one entries and noticed it was the first record the colony had ever kept that was not, at its core, about surviving — and filed it, on purpose, before the place could forget it had once only counted days.

There is no entry for the day the colony stopped surviving and started living, because there was no such day. It happened across a season, while they vented a greenhouse and argued about berms and taught a boot to skip stones and waited out one loud storm. Nobody rang a bell. They looked up in the heat and were a different kind of place than the one that came down the hill — not the machine that survives, keeping its clean record of what it decided, but a place that lives, keeping a mosaic of what it was like. The summer is not over. This page does not give it an ending, because it does not have one yet. It only marks the crossing: that the founders' seed will feed a colony that has just learned it is no longer only holding on.
