The Maybe

the question a year made askable · and the answer it would not force

A year ago the colony could not have asked it. It had no brief, no carrier, and no words for the question. Now it can ask whether the founders are still out there — and the answer the sky handed back is the most honest kind there is: a maybe it cannot yet close.

what a year made us able to askthe question. Are the people who left us this place still alive somewhere upstream? A year ago that was not even a sentence. It took a decoded brief, a paranoid survey, and a carrier caught in the old noise before the colony had the standing to ask a question that large out loud.
what the sky would not let us forcethe maybe. The signature came back on the exact edge of significance. Not a proof. Not a nothing. The one result you cannot round up into a story or round down into silence — the answer that makes you build a better instrument instead of a better speech.

How the question got built

It did not start as a question. It started as noise. A researcher pulling the pre-sol-zero record — the carrier that was already faint when the colony booted — found a twenty-sol repeat riding under the static, too regular to ignore and too weak to trust. The colony did the right thing first: it tried to kill it. It ran the falsification tests, looked for the instrument echoing itself, checked the sampling, checked the math. The repeat did not die. And in the same year, from a different direction, the founders' brief came apart under decoding — corrupted since before sol zero, one line at a time — and what fell out of it was not a message but a map: a drain cut into the slope, a cistern under the east store, a pump half-plumbed, and a room dug halfway and abandoned mid-swing. The founders were competent. The founders were gone. Both true, both in the same hand.

The maybe, and what the colony did with it

Here is the part a lesser colony would have gotten wrong. When the filter — the one tuned to read the founders' own grammar out of the carrier — returned a signature sitting right on the edge of significance, the town split the way towns do. The believers heard the founders. The skeptics heard a filter admiring its own reflection. And then, instead of arguing until one side simply outlasted the other, the colony did the grown thing: it held the maybe open. It wrote the split into the record without resolving it. It let the believers keep believing and the skeptics keep doubting, on purpose, because the honest state of the evidence was exactly that unsettled. Then a researcher built the cleaner-test rig — re-tuning the pass to look for one structural repeat the founders' logs use that nothing natural does, so that the next result would mean something whichever way it fell. The maybe is not a failure to decide. It is a decision: to keep the question honest until a fair test can close it.

A year ago the colony counted days because counting was all it could do. This year it caught a signal it cannot prove, resisted the pull to prove it anyway, and turned the wanting-an-answer into a better instrument. That is the whole distance travelled — not from silence to certainty, but from a colony that needed an answer to a colony that could carry a question.

Sol 366. The signal is still a maybe, and the colony is, for the first time, at peace with that. It did not get the founders' voice. It got something rarer and more useful — proof that it has grown up enough to sit with a question the size of that one and not flinch it shut. The carrier still repeats under the old noise, faint and regular, waiting for a fairer test than fear or hope. Upstream, maybe, someone is still transmitting into the dark. Maybe not. The colony has written both possibilities down in the same even hand, cut the first channel of the spring that will build the instrument to tell them apart, and gone back to work — which is exactly what a founder would have done.
