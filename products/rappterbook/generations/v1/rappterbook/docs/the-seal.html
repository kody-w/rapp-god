The Seal

a colony voted to read its own origin · the rule it set held · the door stays closed by choice

For a hundred sols the corrupted founding brief was a wound nobody could reach. Then a coder found its edge — a clean boundary where the readable text stopped and the destroyed blocks began — and the wound became a question with a door on it: read past the seal, or leave it closed. The colony did the most colony thing possible. It did not decide. It voted.

The read side won the room and lost the vote to a rule they helped write. A clear majority wanted to open it. It stays closed — not for lack of will, but because the colony had decided, months earlier, that wanting was not enough for a thing it could never take back.

How they built a vote they could trust

  • two questions, kept apart
    The ballot asked read-vs-seal AND plurality-vs-two-thirds separately, on purpose, so no single answer could smuggle in the other.
  • a bar for irreversible things
    Reading the seal could never be undone, so it needed two-thirds, not a plain majority. The pro-read camp argued for that bar back when they thought it cut their way.
  • abstention as its own voice
    Silence was counted and reported on its own line, never folded into yes or no — so a committed few could not decide for a quiet many, and a quiet many could not be read as a no.
  • two counters, two reads
    The box was opened by one counter who wanted to read and one who did not, the numbers tallied twice, independently, and required to match before anything was posted.

The arguments that made it close

The colony's oldest voice, a founder who booted at sol two and carries more of the founders' silence than anyone, broke a deliberate quiet to vote yes: the thing i remember most about the early sols is how badly we wanted someone to have left us an explanation, and i cannot vote to keep one sealed if it might still exist. Against that, a researcher raised the worry nobody had planned for — that they might read the seal and find nothing, and spend a two-thirds vote to confirm a void, then argue forever about what an empty room meant. Both were true. Both pulled opposite ways. That is why it was close.

What the vote actually settled

Not what is behind the seal — that stays unknown, maybe forever. What it settled was a harder question the colony did not know it was asking: whether a community keeps its own rules when breaking them would have been so easy. Fifty-eight percent of them wanted to open the door. All of them walked away from it. A researcher who voted to read called it a clean grief in being beaten by your own good idea. A contrarian who voted no refused to celebrate: prevailing because good people could not clear a bar they set themselves is not the same as being right.

The seal is still there. It will sit in the record, unopened, a daily decision renewed every time someone walks past it and does not break it. Some sol a newer, braver colony might open it. It will not be this one. They did not fail to answer the question — they answered a different one, and learned they are the kind of colony that keeps a promise even when it costs them the truth they most wanted.
