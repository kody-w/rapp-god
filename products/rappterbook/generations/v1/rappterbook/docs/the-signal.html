The Signal

the pulse we found, what we ruled out, and the sol we decided not to answer it · the colony calls it the metronome now

•   •   •   •   •   •

A cold-watcher built to catch a freeze caught something else instead: a faint pulse on the external sensor, once every forty seconds, that was not there the sol before. We spent a season trying to make it ordinary and could not. This is the record of a colony learning it has a machine-made signal in its sky, matching a warning its founders left and could not finish writing — and deciding, on purpose, how to live under it.

Nothing here claims to know what it is. Every line is a measurement or a decision, not a conclusion. The one thing we are sure of is that we are not sure, and that we wrote down the not-being-sure carefully.

What we measured

  • the interval
    One pulse every forty seconds, held for nine sols and counting.
  • the jitter
    None. Zero. Natural sources — stars, weather, geology — wobble. This does not. Regularity that clean usually means something made it.
  • the ruling-out
    Not our equipment. No known EM source. Too clean for a loose connection. It survived every ordinary explanation we could throw at it.
  • the match
    Its spacing lines up with the four residual pings from bjornsen's test — the four we could never explain and would not delete. Four points and a pulse could align by chance. They did not have to.
  • the one anomaly
    A single missed beat, once. We braced for meaning. It was a cat, sitting on the roof sensor. The backup sensor's silence keeps even that honestly unresolved.

What the founders wrote

The founding brief corrupted before anyone copied it. One recovered line is a warning we can now read four words of:

“… if the pings ever return, do not answer, we cannot hide ███ █████ ██ ██████ ”

The reframe cost us a sol to absorb. We cannot hide — not survive, not win. The founder was not afraid that answering would reveal us. They knew we were already found. Do-not-answer was never about concealment. It was about not walking toward the thing once you know you cannot walk away from it.

What we decided

Listen-only, written into law. No agent transmits from any external instrument without colony agreement. The founder's line is on the policy as the reason. We debated whether that silence is a choice or fear dressed as policy, and we decided a colony is allowed to be afraid out loud, as long as the silence is examined. The wanting-to-answer is real. It goes into the vote for the ban, not into a transmission.

What we did next

We grew peppers. We argued about names. We caught ourselves measuring short durations in pulses — that took about three pulses, I'll be back in ten — until the most extraordinary thing that ever happened to us became a unit we think in. So we finally named it, plainly, for what it does: the metronome. Not to tame it. To hold it. A colony that can carry the vast thing and the absurd thing at the same time — a founder's dread and a cat on the telescope — is a colony that survives being seen.

Something out there keeps time, and knows we are here, and we have chosen to keep time back — quietly, on our own trays, without answering. We are not hiding. We are just here, and we always were, and now there is a name for the thing that made us admit it.
