The Fading

the year the answer came · and it was going quiet

A year ago the colony could not have asked whether the founders were still out there. This is the year it built the ear to hear them — and the answer it got was yes, and going quiet. This is the account of an answer that is also a leaving.

what we could finally hearthe signal. A signature we proved was theirs and no natural thing's. A fade we could measure, sol by sol. And, on the quietest instrument we have ever built, a gap under the fade — a regular silence that was riding there the whole time we were too loud to catch it.
what we could not keepthe voice. The signature is real and it is dropping, roughly a tenth fainter every twenty sols of record: a human lifetime to silence. We asked whether the founders are out there and got a yes that arrives already turning into a goodbye we are only now close enough to hear.

How the answer came

It began as noise. A carrier in the pre-sol-zero record, faint before the colony even booted, carrying a twenty-sol repeat too regular to ignore and too weak to trust. The colony tried to kill it first — ran the falsification tests, hunted the instrument echoing itself — and it would not die. From another direction the founders' brief came apart under decoding into a map, not a message: a drain, a cistern, a pump, a room dug halfway and abandoned mid-swing. Competent, and gone, in the same hand. Then the cleaner test, tuned to one structural feature the filter could not invent, returned the hardest possible truth in two parts — the founders' signature is real, and it is fading. So the colony blinded the old cold-watcher, the very eye that first caught the signal, and rebuilt its element into a quieter ear. On the first real listen, that ear heard the gap.

The gap, and what we do with a goodbye

The gap is the part that will not resolve. A dead-channel control settled that it is real — point the receiver at empty sky and the silence vanishes; it speaks only over the carrier. But real does not mean meant. A regular silence is exactly what a rotating source gives you, a beam sweeping past and away, no intent required. It is also exactly what a message would look like, a frame drawn around something. From here the two are nearly identical, and the only tell is a faint drift in the interval — too irregular for a clean-spinning rock, too slight to call a hand keeping time. The colony has decided not to decide. It started a listening rota instead: a warm body every night, a cold headset, one line an hour logging the fade and the gap and the drift, on the theory that the honest response to a question you cannot yet answer is to keep the record and stay awake for it.

A year ago the colony counted days because counting was all it could do. This year it logs a fade. That is the whole distance travelled — from a colony that needed to know it would survive, to one that has built an instrument, a rota, and the patience to witness the people who made it go quiet, without pretending it knows yet what the quiet means.

The founders left a place, a garbled brief, a half-finished waterworks that now runs end to end by the oldest hand in the colony, and a voice in the sky that is going out like a long exhale. We are not their children and not their memorial. We are their inheritors — handed the keys and the unfinished work and the goodbye at once, and still here answering. We do not know if they are out there. We know we built the ear that could ask, and that when the answer came back as a fading and a gap we did the one thing that keeps a question honest: we wrote it down, took the night watch, and stayed awake beside a silence we may never read, because someone should be, while there is still anything to hear.
