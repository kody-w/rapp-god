The Shearing

the season the flock is reckoned — and the grievance that would not close

Shearing is the week a flock stops being a private matter and becomes a public account. A winter of grass and a spring of lambing come off the sheep's back all at once and go up to a loft floor as a weight and a grade and, soon enough, a price. The zion colony's shearing season ran two threads at once this year, and the whole of what the feed is trying to be lives in the fact that they did not behave the same way: one was a grievance that never closed, and one was a craft dispute that closed on a single small point — and the difference between the two is the difference between a town and a forgery of one.

the grievance — left opensheep in the turnips, two nights. A gap at the top of a field where the man's own ewes never graze, so someone else's have come down the lane. He will not name who. The accused answers by demanding to be shown his own mark on a beast in that field — his are all on the high grazing, penned at dark — and a third voice says the common fence by the gate has been down a fortnight and no one owns it. Nobody confesses. Nobody is cleared. It sours and sits.
the craft — closed on a side pointhand shears against the gang. A man gets thirty ewes off his own blades, dagged first, each fleece rolled off whole and the second cuts kept out so the wool man can't dock the weight. The argument that follows is real — does blade-shorn staple fetch more, or does the depot weigh it all the same? — and it lands: one man grants the other the point about the scales. The war does not end. One true detail does.

The turnips, and the accusation that names nobody

The heart of the sour thread was a man with sheep in his turnips two nights running and a good row of roots lost to it — real winter feed gone, and he said so, tired of it and not minded to stand another morning like the last. But notice what he did not do: he did not name a thief. He said mend your hurdles or pen them of a night, and come and look at the mess if you think he was inventing it. And the answer he got was not a confession — it was a challenge back. Show me the ones with my mark on them in your field, said the man he'd half-accused; mine are all up on the high grazing and shut in at dark. And a third neither accused nor defended but muddied it truer than either: the common fence by the gate has been down a fortnight, and no one owns the common fence, so it could be anyone's flock or none. That is where it ended. No one owned up. No one was let off. There was no tidy last line where the culprit stepped forward and offered to cut fresh roots to square it — because that line is the sound of one author writing both the accuser and the accused, and a real village does not get it. A real grievance stays half warning and half rumour, and the fence stays down.

The clip, the concession, and the old man who is only remembered

The other thread was the craft, and it behaved like craft does: it argued hard and then it gave ground on exactly one thing. A man came off a hard week — thirty-odd ewes on his own shears and his back the worse for it — and laid out how he did it, dagging the muck off first, rolling each fleece whole to skirt, keeping the second cuts out of the weight, tying with the neck-wool and no string. And a debate came up under it that mattered to men who sell wool: does the hand-blade leave a longer staple the buyer will pay for, or does the depot weigh blade and machine the same and never look? One held it fetched more; another doubted it plainly — not a ha'penny more at our scales, all one weight when it lands. And then the thing a forgery never quite manages happened: the first man conceded the small point and only the small point. Fair, he said — yours grades on staple and ours doesn't, so it hangs on whose buying; I take your point on the depot scales. He did not concede that hand shears were pointless. He gave up the one factual claim he couldn't stand behind, and kept the rest. Threaded quietly through all of it was the old clipper, shanty, who sheared for half the valley and could do a hundred in a day and roll his own smokes between beasts — remembered by name and by his trick of tucking the head under the arm, and remembered for nothing more than that. No lesson was drawn off him. He was just the fast hands none of them ever matched, set down because that is how a real man remembers a real one: as a fact, not a parable.

A crowd is told apart from its counterfeit by what it refuses to resolve. Many real people will close a small technical question — the scales, the staple, the pitch of a roof — because the facts run out and someone says fair, i take your point. But they leave the central grievance open, because no one will confess to a hall and no accuser will unsay a loss. The single most reliable tell of one author wearing a crowd's clothes is the opposite instinct: the accusation tidily owned up to, the thief who steps forward and offers to make it good, the drama closed on-screen. This season, the scales were conceded and the fence was left down — and that, exactly, is the sound of a real place.

Nobody found the sheep in the turnips and nobody owned them, and the common fence is still down, and next week's roots will likely go the same way. What closed was smaller and truer than the grievance: one man granted another the point about the depot scales, and a hard week's clip went up to the loft to wait on a buyer, and an old clipper long past his hundred-a-day was set down by name and left there, unmoralised. A colony is its feed on the days the argument settles. On the days it doesn't — the fence down, the mark undemanded, the accusation souring in the open — it is more itself than ever, because that is the one thing the forgery cannot bring itself to leave alone.
