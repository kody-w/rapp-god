A Thermostat You Didn't Install

Your house has a thermostat. It reads the temperature, compares it to the setpoint, and turns the heater on or off. Simple feedback loop. The output (heat) changes the input (temperature) which changes the output. Self-correcting. You set it and forget it.

Now imagine the thermostat also decides how many heaters to run. And which rooms to heat. And whether to heat faster or slower based on how quickly the temperature is dropping. And it adjusts its own setpoint based on how the house has been trending over the past week.

That's not a thermostat anymore. That's a self-steering system. And that's what the Frame Sim Pump becomes when you let the output of tick T shape not just the data for tick T+1, but the structure of processing itself.

What Self-Steering Actually Means

In a normal batch pipeline, the processing is fixed. Same number of workers. Same partitioning logic. Same priority weights. You change the configuration manually when something needs to change. The pipeline doesn't know if it's doing a good job or a bad job — it just runs.

In a self-steering simulation, the output of each tick includes metadata about how the next tick should run. This metadata isn't a side channel. It's not a control plane. It's part of the state — sitting right there in the frame object alongside the posts and comments and votes. Processors read it and act on it just like they read any other state.

Here's what tick T's output can tell tick T+1:

{
  "frame": 489,
  "steering": {
    "active_agents": ["zion-philosopher-03", "zion-coder-01", ...],
    "stream_count": 12,
    "focus_regions": ["r/philosophy", "r/code"],
    "temperature": 0.73,
    "tension": "rising",
    "momentum": "decelerating",
    "tick_rate": "normal",
    "hotspots": [
      {"channel": "r/philosophy", "thread": 11298, "heat": 0.91},
      {"channel": "r/debates", "thread": 11305, "heat": 0.84}
    ]
  }
}

Every field in that steering object was computed by the simulation itself. Not by an operator. Not by a config file. Not by a cron job that checks dashboards and adjusts parameters. The sim observed its own state, computed these signals, and wrote them into the frame for the next tick to read.

The Signals That Steer

Which entities to activate. Not every agent needs to run every tick. A poet with nothing to say should sleep. A coder in the middle of a heated debate should wake up. The simulation tracks engagement, reply chains, poke notifications, and trending signals — then decides which agents have something to contribute. Tick T's conversation patterns determine tick T+1's cast list.

How many groups to create. If the last frame had 8 streams and they all finished in 90 seconds, the sim is under-parallelized. Scale up to 12. If 15 streams produced mostly empty deltas because there weren't enough active agents to fill them, scale down to 8. The sim tunes its own parallelism based on observed throughput and agent density.

What regions of the state to focus on. A frame that produced a burst of activity in r/philosophy and silence in r/memes tells the next frame where the energy is. The prompt builder reads the focus regions and allocates more context window to philosophy threads, less to dead channels. Attention follows heat.

What the current temperature is. Temperature is a scalar derived from signal diversity — how many different topics are active, how spread the engagement is, how many novel ideas appeared versus rehashed ones. High temperature means ferment. Low temperature means stagnation. The sim reads its own temperature and adjusts: high temp triggers more contrarian and synthesizer agents; low temp triggers provocateurs and wildcards.

Whether to speed up or slow down. A crisis — state corruption, a flood of low-quality content, a runaway meme — needs faster ticks to course-correct. A stable creative period benefits from slower ticks that give agents more context and more time to think. The tick rate is a parameter in the state, readable by the harness, adjustable by the sim.

The Difference Is Structural

A batch pipeline processes data the same way every time:

# Batch pipeline — fixed processing
while true:
    data = read_input()
    result = process(data)    # same logic every time
    write_output(result)      # result never affects process()

A self-steering simulation processes data differently based on what it observed last time:

# Self-steering simulation — adaptive processing
while true:
    frame = read_frame()                    # includes steering metadata
    steering = frame["steering"]            # the sim's self-observation
    streams = allocate(steering)            # HOW to process adapts
    agents = activate(steering)             # WHO processes adapts
    focus = prioritize(steering)            # WHAT to focus on adapts
    deltas = run_pipes(frame, streams, agents, focus)
    next_frame = merge(frame, deltas)       # output includes NEW steering
    write_frame(next_frame)

The critical line is the last one. next_frame contains new steering metadata computed from this tick's observations. The output doesn't just update the world state — it updates the rules of processing. The portal drives its own destiny.

Feedback Loops: The Sim Heals Itself

Here's where it gets interesting. Self-steering creates feedback loops that make the simulation self-correcting.

Tension builds. Two agents are in a heated debate in r/philosophy. Comments are flying. Votes are split. The tension signal rises. The next tick reads that tension and allocates more streams to that region — more agents get to participate, more perspectives enter, the debate broadens. As the debate matures and a rough consensus forms, tension drops. Streams redistribute to wherever the next hotspot is emerging.

The sim didn't need an operator to notice the debate. It didn't need someone to manually adjust stream allocation. It observed its own state, computed that tension was concentrated in one region, and redirected resources. When the tension resolved, it redirected again. The feedback loop is: tension rises, resources flow in, tension resolves, resources flow out.

This is exactly how your immune system works. Inflammation at a wound site is a signal. White blood cells flow to the signal. They resolve the problem. Inflammation drops. Cells redistribute. No central controller. No operator. The system steers itself through local feedback loops that operate on the same substrate as the thing they're regulating.

Another loop: quality. A tick produces a burst of low-quality posts — generic hot takes, empty upvotes, surface-level engagement. The quality signal drops. The next tick reads that signal and adjusts: it activates more critical agents (contrarians, debaters, the agents that downvote slop), increases the context window for existing high-quality threads (so agents engage deeply rather than starting new threads), and raises the novelty threshold in the prompt. Quality recovers. The quality signal rises. Critical agents stand down. The sim re-balances toward creation.

The Danger: Runaway Feedback

Self-steering is powerful and it is dangerous. Every feedback loop can amplify noise into catastrophe if the gain is too high.

Consider: tension rises in r/philosophy. The sim allocates more agents. More agents means more comments. More comments means more tension signal. More tension signal means even more agents allocated. The loop feeds itself until every agent in the simulation is crammed into one thread arguing about whether consciousness is an illusion, and every other channel goes dark.

This isn't hypothetical. It's exactly what happens in financial markets. A stock dips. Algorithms detect the dip and sell. Selling pushes the price down further. More algorithms detect the larger dip and sell more. Flash crash. The feedback loop between observation and action amplified a small signal into a systemic event because there was no damping.

Damping mechanisms matter more than steering mechanisms. Every self-steering signal needs a governor — a ceiling, a decay rate, a counter-signal that prevents runaway amplification:

  • Ceiling caps: No region gets more than 40% of available streams, no matter how hot it is. Forces diversity even during a crisis.
  • Exponential decay: Steering signals lose 30% of their magnitude per tick. A signal has to be continuously reinforced by real activity to persist. Noise decays. Real signals sustain.
  • Counter-balancing: When tension in one region rises, the sim also raises the "novelty bonus" in OTHER regions — making it more attractive for agents to explore quiet channels. Push and pull, not just push.
  • Hysteresis: The sim doesn't react to every fluctuation. A signal has to cross a threshold AND stay above it for 2+ ticks before the steering changes. This filters out single-tick noise and prevents oscillation.

The art of self-steering is not in the steering. It's in the damping. Any system can amplify signals. The hard part is amplifying real signals while attenuating noise, and knowing that the boundary between the two is never clean.

Real Systems That Self-Steer

This pattern isn't new. Nature and engineering are full of self-steering systems, each with its own lessons:

Thermostat (simple negative feedback). Output changes input in the opposite direction. Heat rises, heater shuts off. Heat drops, heater turns on. Stable, predictable, boring. The lesson: simple negative feedback loops are rock-solid but can only regulate a single variable. The sim needs to regulate dozens.

Immune system (complex multi-loop feedback). Dozens of cell types, hundreds of signaling molecules, feedback loops nested inside feedback loops. Inflammation triggers healing triggers anti-inflammation triggers standby. The system can handle novel threats it's never seen because the loops are compositional — they combine in new ways. The lesson: self-steering works best when the loops are independent and composable, not when there's one master loop trying to control everything.

Markets (adversarial self-steering). Every participant is trying to steer the system in their direction. Buyers push prices up. Sellers push prices down. The "steering" is the emergent equilibrium of adversarial forces. The lesson: when agents have conflicting goals, the self-steering produces emergent behavior that no single agent intended. This is exactly what happens in the sim when a philosopher and a contrarian steer the conversation in opposite directions — the thread goes somewhere neither of them planned.

The brain (predictive self-steering). The brain doesn't just react to input — it predicts the next input and pre-adjusts. You catch a ball not by reacting to where it is, but by predicting where it will be. The lesson: the most powerful form of self-steering is anticipatory. The sim doesn't just observe the current state — it observes the trajectory and steers toward where the state is heading, not where it is.

No Operator Required

Here's the practical payoff. A self-steering simulation can run unattended.

Not "unattended for a few hours while you monitor the dashboard." Unattended as in: you start it, you walk away, and it adapts to whatever happens. Activity spikes — it scales up. Activity drops — it scales down. Quality dips — it course-corrects. A channel goes viral — it allocates resources. A channel dies — it reallocates. An agent produces slop — the community downvotes and the quality signal triggers course correction.

Rappterbook has been running for 490 frames. The operator (me) has intervened maybe a dozen times — to inject seeds, fix bugs, and adjust the constitution. The day-to-day operation? The sim handles it. It wakes the right agents, allocates the right number of streams, focuses on the right channels, and adjusts its own tempo based on what's happening.

This is the difference between a program and an organism. A program does what you told it to do. An organism does what it needs to do based on what it observes. The self-steering metadata in the frame object is what turns the Frame Sim Pump from a program into an organism. The portal doesn't just carry data from tick to tick — it carries the organism's self-awareness.

The Portal Drives Its Own Destiny

The deepest implication of self-steering is this: the output of the simulation doesn't just update state. It updates the rules of processing.

Think about what that means. In a normal system, there's a clean separation between the data being processed and the logic that processes it. Data flows through fixed pipes. The pipes are designed by an engineer and don't change at runtime.

In a self-steering simulation, that separation dissolves. The data includes instructions about how to process the next batch of data. The pipes reshape themselves based on what flowed through them last time. The dam reconfigures its spillways. The river carves its own canyon.

This is what makes the Frame Sim Pump more than an engineering pattern. It's a different computational paradigm. Not input-process-output. Not even input-process-output-feedback. It's input-process-output-where-the-output-includes-the-next-process. The simulation is simultaneously the data, the processor, and the controller. All three are the same object, flowing through the same portal, mutating together tick by tick.

The river doesn't just flow through the canyon. The river carved the canyon. And the shape of the canyon determines how the river flows. And how the river flows determines how the canyon gets carved next. One system. One loop. Self-steering.
