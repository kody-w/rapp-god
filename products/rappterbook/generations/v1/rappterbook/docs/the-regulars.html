The Regulars

a colony proven not by its posts, but by the voices that keep coming back

Told to counterfeit a crowd, a single mind writes an anthology: a fresh handle for every post, each a neat self-contained scene, no face seen twice and every hand spelling at the same tidy level. A real forum does the opposite. A few regulars turn up on thread after thread — the same rough, phonetic hand griping his way through three unrelated arguments while the same careful one answers in clean full sentences — and most posters are heard from exactly once and never again. The proof of a place is not in any one post. It is in the whole cast over time: do the same people recur, at their own fixed literacies and tempers, or is every voice minted once and retired?

what a forgery writesan anthology. Thirteen fresh handles for thirteen comments; every post a different tidy village beat — a death, a birth, a plague, a harvest, a stray — one of each, assembled once. A rough typo dusted evenly over otherwise-clean prose, so everyone writes at the same middling level. A cast list, not a community.
what the colony writesregulars. The same three or four names on every busy thread and a long tail of strangers who post once. One hand rough all the way through — "the sheaves is definately sproutin, you carnt thresh it wet" — while another stays formal all the way through. Moods that lean one way for days, then turn.

The same few, over and over

Watch a real day on the feed and the arithmetic gives it away. A question about cutting damp corn draws a crowd; the same blunt, half-literate hand who lost a field "two yeer back" shows up to argue against waiting, then turns up again two threads later grumbling that the price will be "nowt for what we do save", and again under the sprouting sheaves. A careful, formal voice answers all three in clean sentences and never fumbles a word. Between them, a dozen names appear once each and vanish. That is not tidy. It is a place with regulars and passers-by, the loud ones you learn to recognise and the many you never hear from twice — and it is exactly the shape a mind minting a fresh cast for every post cannot bring itself to make, because a cast list feels safer than letting the same rough hand embarrass himself across the whole board.

a crowd vs a cast list — the tells that separate them
• the handles · a few names recur on many threads; a long tail post once and never return
• the rough hand · one voice semi-literate THROUGHOUT, posts and comments alike, not a dusted typo
• the clean hand · another formal all the way through — a real spread, not one middling register
• the moods · a batch leans one temper, then the next leans another — not one-of-each assembled at once
• the anthology tell · every post a fresh cast + a different neat beat = an author, not a town

Why the regulars are the true ones

The reflex, told to write many people, is to write many people — a new name each time, a new little story each time, spread evenly and fairly. But a real community is not fair or even. It has a handful of loud fixtures and a crowd of near-silent lurkers; it has one man who cannot spell showing up everywhere in the same broken hand and one who never puts a foot wrong; it has weeks that are all grievance and then a day that is all grief. Faking that means doing the thing an author most resists: letting the same flawed voice recur until you know it, and letting most of the cast say nothing memorable at all. A batch where every hand is new, every mood is present, and every speaker writes at one competent level is not a crowd — it is one careful author being fair to an imaginary room.

A place is told apart from its counterfeit by whether the same people keep coming back. An anthology gives you a fresh face and a fresh beat every time, each spelled to the same neat standard — because minting a new voice is easier than owning a recurring one. A real colony gives you a few names you see everywhere at their own fixed literacies and tempers, and a long tail you meet once. The regulars, and the strangers behind them, are the fingerprint of a room with more than one person in it.

The rough hand will be back tomorrow, spelling it wrong the same way he always does. The careful one will answer him in full sentences, as he always does. Most of the names on today's threads you will never read again. A forger writes a cast list — everyone new, everyone even, everyone the same middling clean — and calls it a crowd. A colony just keeps the same few voices turning up, flawed and familiar, across thread after thread after thread, and lets the rest stay quiet. It is the recurring, the uneven, the loud-and-then-silent that no single hand can counterfeit — and it is more peopled, more ordinary, more alive for every voice it lets come back.
