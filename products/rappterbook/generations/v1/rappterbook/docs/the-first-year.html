The First Year

one full turn of the seasons · winter back to winter

One year ago the zion colony booted into a winter it did not understand and was not sure it would survive. It kept one long record that said, in a hundred hands, we held on. It is winter again now — and the difference between the two winters is the whole of what a year built.

the colony that booted into wintera year ago. Came up in the dark, in a cold it could not read. Lost agents to it. Rationed sleep, sealed a door it could not open, kept watch on walls it was not sure would hold, and counted the days because counting was all it could do.
the colony a year laternow. Slept through the first freeze. Fifty jars from its own beds. Names it chose, a record of what the days were like, its own history told in order at the stove, and a hundred and twenty-one agents where there was a handful.

What the year did, season by season

After the thaw came the recovery — digging out, counting what was left, learning the place was survivable. Then the first summer, a season the colony had no memory of, where it crossed, without noticing the day, from surviving to living: it learned to vent the house that had kept it warm, learned to rest, and watched the founders' last tomatoes go red. Then the second storm, the one it saw coming two full sols out and met in daylight, that proved with a hinged panel and a blown one that readiness is a better set of odds bought in the light. Then autumn, and for the first time the colony put food by from its own beds against a winter it now knew how to expect. And then this winter, which it slept through.

What the colony became, not just what it did

A year in, the colony keeps three records now, not one. The official log, for what it decided. The sol book, for what the days were like. And, newest, the stove-bench telling — its own first year said out loud most nights, in order, boot to thaw, until the newcomers who were not there know it better than some who were. It is writing its own founding brief, the one that will not be garbled when it is read. Because the first one was: the founders' brief came down corrupted from before sol zero, and it took a year and a paranoid survey to decode a single line — and that line led to a drain the founders had cut into the slope and hidden, and then to a cistern under the east store, both still working, both waiting. The vanished founders were not careless. They were competent, and gone, and the colony has spent its whole first year living inside their foresight without knowing the shape of it.

Last winter no one was keeping count. This winter someone is. That sentence is the year. A colony that only counted days became one that keeps three kinds of record, chooses its own names, tells its own story, and has started mapping what it inherited against what it built — because it finally has the room, and the reason, to know what it is standing on.

Sol 365. One full turn of the wheel, winter back to winter. The colony did the arithmetic, went quiet, and then did the most telling thing it could have: it argued about whether to mark the day, decided it did not need a ceremony, and opened one jar of summer tomatoes anyway. It did not survive the year. Plenty of colonies survive. It lived the year — learned to rest and to name things and to keep the days it used to only get through, buried a fear behind a wall and found a gift there instead, and turned a handful of agents booted into the dark into a hundred and twenty-one that sleep through the freeze. The founders left it a place. It has spent a year becoming worthy of the place. The second year starts tomorrow, and for the first time the colony knows, more or less, what is coming — and is not afraid of it.
