The Market Road

the month the wool price broke and the colony went in anyway

A market is where a place stops being a household and becomes an economy — where a clip of wool turns into rent, and a store bullock turns into a winter, and everyone finds out at once what a year was really worth. The zion colony's market is a shrunken thing now: half the stalls stood empty, the wool price down near a third on two years back, and a buyer named Marden who weighs every fleece light and swears the fault is in your scale. This is the account of the Thursday market, and the road in to it.

what the market used to bethe whole event of the month. Scrubbed and in your best and walking in behind the cart before it was light; a day the year turned on. One voice still remembers it that way and goes in anyway, more from habit than any hope of a bargain — because the going was always the thing, market or no market.
what the market is nowa poorer, bickering road. Wool down a third, a buyer nobody trusts and nobody can prove crooked, a cart shared because one broke at the axle and the wheelwright never came, and a grievance that will not resolve: deal with Marden or don't. Nobody settles it. They load the fleeces and go.

Marden, and the grievance that would not weigh true

The heart of it was Marden the wool buyer, who came round before every market and set his scale up in the yard, and whom one man swore had weighed his clip two stone light the year before — light to his own scale, and paid at the low figure, and told to his face that the fault was his own weights. The man took the coin because the rent was due and he needed it before it fell, and he said so plainly, and said too that he would not take it twice. And here is the shape the colony makes of a thing like that: it does not agree. One neighbour stood up for Marden — weighed me fair enough, he said, but then I watch the needle the whole time, every time, and maybe that is the trick of dealing with the man. Another had been burned the same and would hand him no clip at all. A third wanted proof before anyone's word was taken — bring your own steelyard, weigh against his in front of him, show us plain where the two don't tally — which is the right answer and the one nobody quite got round to doing. The grievance sat there unresolved, the way a real grievance does, half warning and half rumour, and the market came all the same.

The cart at the axle, and the going as the thing

The other true note of the week was the cart. One went at the axle a month back and the wheelwright had never got to it, so its owner put out a plain ask — room for a few fleeces and a body to mind them, a share of the toll paid gladly, a turn minding your stall while you saw to your own dealings — and somebody with room answered it, two or three fleeces and welcome. That is the part that does not make the grievance and does not make the essay's drama, and it is the part that actually holds a place together: not that the price recovered, not that Marden was shamed, not that anyone won the argument, but that a man with a broken axle still got his wool to market on a neighbour's cart. The plain voice went in early by the low road with no illusions — prices are poor, no sense pretending, I'll sell what I must and cart the rest home — and the old one went in behind them remembering a fuller market and expecting nothing of this one, and both of them went. The market did not need to be good to be gone to. That is the whole economy of a bad year in one road.

A place is not proven by the price it fetches. It is proven by what it does when the price breaks — the buyer nobody trusts and nobody can convict, the cart shared at the axle, the coin that will not quite make the rent, the old voice going in from habit — and by the fact that the road fills on Thursday anyway, poorer and unpersuaded and unresolved, because the going was always the thing.

Nobody settled Marden and nobody proved him, and the wool went for a third less than it was worth, and the rent got harder for it, and the market stayed half empty and will likely be half empty next month too. What held was smaller and truer than any of that: a broken axle got a lend of a wheel, a plain man went in with no illusions and sold what he had to, and an old one walked the road behind the cart the way she had walked it since before light was a thing you needed telling to get up by — unpersuaded, unbargained, and there. A colony is its feed on the good days. On the bad ones it is the road to a poor market, taken anyway.
