The Second Storm

the one the colony saw coming · and what it proved

The first storm came in winter, in the dark, unseen, and nearly ended the colony. The second came in summer — and the colony knew about it for two full sols before it hit. That gap, that two-sol warning, is the whole distance this place has travelled. One storm nearly ended a colony that was only surviving. The next one it prepared for in daylight, and it held.

the first stormwinter, unseen. It arrived in the dark with no warning. The colony rode it out not knowing if the walls would hold, lost the east berms, and spent weeks digging out and longer than that afraid. It survived on luck and a full barrel.
the second stormsummer, foreseen. The glass fell two sols out. The colony battened in daylight, moved its records to high ground, locked its panels into a test, and waited. It lost a pane of glass and a berm. Nobody was hurt.

The tell

The colony did not see the second storm coming because of one instrument. It saw it three ways at once, and that redundancy is itself the thing that changed. The barometer dropped four points and kept falling. The cats — the baron and the pretender, a week into a standoff over a heating vent — called a truce and sat side by side on the shed roof, both facing east. And bjorn came in from the pool early, because the fish had stopped biting and the light had gone yellow, and started moving the loose gear under cover before anyone asked. A colony that once logged cat behavior as noise cleared its benches on the strength of it. Then the sky did what all three had said it would.

The proof

The second storm was not only survived — it was used. For weeks the colony had argued about whether to hinge its greenhouse panels so they could vent under pressure, or leave them fixed. It could not settle the argument with opinion, so it settled it with the storm: two panels hinged, two left fixed, same wall, same wind, labeled, and nobody allowed to touch them once it started. The storm read the experiment out:

the panel result — filed with the failure photos and the pressure log
• hinged panel, east pair — vented the pressure, held, no crack
• hinged panel, west pair — vented the pressure, held, no crack
• fixed panel, west — held, this time
• fixed panel, east — blew in at the second hour, along the seam

Two hinged intact, one fixed intact, one fixed gone. That is not a story anymore, it is a result. The colony did not come through because it guessed right; it came through because it had turned a fear into a test and let the storm grade it. The last fixed panel was hinged the morning the store reopened. The comparison is closed. There is nothing left on the north run to fail.

What it cost

One pane of glass and a slumped berm that did not breach, because the drainage channel dug back in spring ran full all night and did its job. Three shutters. No structural loss to any bunk, no stock lost behind the supply valves the crews had shut, and no injuries — though it came close. The north bunk shutter lost its top hinge at the second hour and started to peel with the whole bunk asleep behind it, and two riggers went out into the dark on a line and strapped it down half-blind. It held. We were ready for this storm and still nearly lost it, one of them wrote, and I keep remembering the ones we did not see coming. Readiness is not safety. It is only a better set of odds, bought in daylight.

Three sols after the storm, the colony voted on its winter reserve rule — and the storm was in the room. The proposal was to cut the summer reserve to five days. The bench looked at five days with the wreckage still fresh and balked, and settled on seven. A summer that can do this earns a seven. The storm did not just test the panels; it wrote itself into the law.

Same storm, different colony

Put the two storms side by side and almost nothing about the weather changed. The wind was the wind. What changed was the place it hit. A year ago a storm like that found a colony that was only holding on, and it took the berms and nearly took the rest. This one found a colony that reads the sky three ways, prepares in daylight, moves its own memory to high ground, and turns its fears into experiments — and it took a pane of glass. Same storm. Different colony.

The first summer taught the colony it was no longer only surviving. The second storm was the test of that lesson, and the lesson held. Not because the colony became safe — it did not, and it knows it, and it kept the seventh day of reserve to prove it knows it. It held because a place that has crossed from surviving to living does not stop preparing; it prepares better, in the light, together, and then it loses a pane of glass where it used to lose a season. The storms will keep coming. The colony has stopped meeting them in the dark.
