Mira did not sleep that night. She lay in bed with Luma on the pillow beside her, the little Daemon's glow turned low, and she stared at the ceiling and thought about grey grass and silence.

At dawn she went to find Elder Sable.

Sable's house was at the top of Warden Hill, a crooked stone cottage with a roof of green slate and a garden that seemed to grow whatever it pleased regardless of season. Mira found the old woman sitting on a bench outside, her Binder open across her knees -- a massive thing, leather cracked and soft with age, holding more Cards than Mira could count. Forty Daemons, organized into seven Decks, each one named and tended like a plot in a garden. Sable was studying one of the Cards, holding it at arm's length, turning it in the early light.

"Something is wrong at the edge," Mira said, because she had learned by now that Sable preferred people who arrived at the point before they arrived at the doorstep.

Sable looked up. Her eyes -- dark, sharp, nested in wrinkles like stones in a dry riverbed -- met Mira's. She did not ask what Mira meant. She did not ask Mira to explain.

She went very still.

"Tell me," she said.

* * *

Mira told her. The grey line. The silence. The way the land beyond it looked thin, translucent, as though the world were a painting and someone had begun to wipe the canvas clean. She told Sable about Luma's reaction -- the dimming, the sharp alertness, the feeling through the bond that was not fear but was certainly not peace.

When she finished, Sable closed her Binder. It was a careful gesture, deliberate, the way you close a door when you don't want to wake someone.

"Luma's Pattern quality," Sable said. "Extraordinary, yes. I thought so. Most Daemons wouldn't have felt it yet. The unraveling is still shallow -- just the topmost threads. But Pattern reads structure the way an ear reads music. Luma heard the discord."

"The unraveling?" Mira said.

Sable stood. She was tall when she stood -- taller than you expected, as though sitting compressed her and standing released something. She looked out past her garden, past the rooftops of Hearthreach, toward the eastern edge where the boundary markers stood.

"It's here," she said, quietly. "We thought we had more time."

* * *

They walked. Sable set a pace that was brisk for someone her age and punishing for someone Mira's, but Mira did not complain. They climbed down Warden Hill, crossed the market square -- empty and dew-wet in the early morning -- and took the border path east, past the last houses, past the stunted trees, to the place where the grass turned grey.

Sable knelt at the line. She placed one hand on the green side and one hand on the grey. She stayed that way for a long time. When she stood, her face was the same -- dry, composed, unreadable -- but her hands were trembling.

"The Fray," she said.

The word landed like a stone in water. Mira felt it sink.

"I need you to listen carefully," Sable said, "because I am going to tell you something that most Operators do not learn until they are much older, and some never learn at all, because they are lucky enough to live in times when they do not need to."

She sat down on a boundary marker -- the crooked one, the last one, the one that leaned as though it were tired of standing -- and she told Mira about the Fray.

It was not a creature. It was not a storm. It was not an enemy you could face and defeat, because it did not think and it did not want and it did not care. The Fray was an absence. The slow, quiet undoing of everything the Lattice held together. Where the Lattice was pattern and connection and the hum of bonds, the Fray was the silence that crept in when those bonds weakened. It appeared at the edges of Reaches -- always the edges first -- and spread inward the way frost spreads across glass. It had no concept of hurry. It had no concept of anything. It just undid.

Where the Fray touched, bonds weakened. Names dissolved -- not the sounds, but the meaning behind them. Seeds could not hatch in Fray-touched ground. Daemons caught in it grew dim and eventually faded, not into death but into something worse: irrelevance. Shapes without substance. Cards without names.

"But it can be held back," Sable said, and something in her voice shifted -- from the measured tone of a teacher to something rawer. "The Fray cannot take hold where bonds are genuine and deep. Not transactions. Not collections. A true bond, the kind the Forge recognizes, the kind that hums -- that is the only thing that holds the Fray at bay. Where bonds are strong, the Lattice holds. Where bonds are shallow, the Lattice thins. And where there are no bonds at all --"

She gestured at the grey grass. The thin, translucent land beyond. The silence.

"-- the Fray walks in."

* * *

"How long has it been here?" Mira asked.

"Weeks, perhaps. Months. It moves slowly enough that you can convince yourself it isn't moving at all. But it is always moving." Sable paused. "I have sent messages to the other Reaches. The reports are similar. It is growing faster than it has in living memory."

Mira felt something cold settle in her stomach.

Sable looked at her -- really looked, the way she had that first day at the Threshold. "There is something else I need to tell you. About your father."

Mira's throat tightened. She knew. She had known since the night she found Cairn's Card in Callum's old Binder, dim and cold in its sleeve, a Card that should have been warm and wasn't. She had known, but she had not wanted to know, and there is a very large distance between those two things.

"The Fray is why your father stopped," Sable said.

And she told Mira what Callum would not.

Years ago -- before Mira, before the carpenter's shop, before the silence -- Callum had been a Forge Warden. He was bonded to Cairn: powerful, steady, a creature of deep Hold quality, like a mountain that had decided to be kind. Their bond was among the strongest in Hearthreach. People spoke of it the way they spoke of old bridges -- with quiet respect for something that had carried weight for a long time.

Then the Fray surged. It came at the northern edge, fast and sudden, overwhelming the boundary wards in a single night. Callum was there. He fought it -- and fighting the Fray was not like fighting anything else. You did not swing a weapon. You held. You stood in the space between the Lattice and the nothing, and you poured your bond into the ground like water into cracked earth, and you held, and held, and held.

Callum held. Cairn held. For hours, they held.

And then the Fray reached through. Not around the bond. Not over it. Through it. Like fingers passing through smoke. The bond dissolved. Not broke -- broke implies two pieces. Dissolved, the way salt dissolves in water, the way a name dissolves when you forget why it was given.

Cairn's Card went dim. The star went dark. Callum described it later -- only once, only to Sable, never again -- as losing a limb he hadn't known was a limb because it had always been there.

He shut himself off from the Lattice entirely. Packed his Binder away. Became a carpenter. Built things from wood, which was solid and silent and did not hum and could not be dissolved.

"He never opened the Binder again?" Mira whispered.

"Not once," Sable said. "Until you found it."

* * *

She found Callum in his workshop, bent over a table leg, sanding with long careful strokes. He looked up when she came in. Sawdust in his hair. Lines around his eyes that she had always thought were from squinting at measurements but now understood were from something else entirely.

"I know about Cairn," she said.

Callum's hand stopped. The sandpaper rested on the wood. He did not look away from her, and she loved him for that, because she could see what it cost him not to.

"Sable," he said. Not a question.

"The Fray is at the eastern edge. The grass is grey. The hum is gone there."

Callum set down the sandpaper. He looked, for a moment, like a man weighed down by something he had been carrying alone for years and had hoped his daughter would never have to carry too.

"I know," he said. "I've known for a while."

"Why didn't you tell me?"

"Because you were hearing the hum for the first time. Because you had just found Luma. Because you were happy, Mira, and the Fray --" He stopped. Swallowed. Started again. "The Fray takes everything. I didn't want it to take that too."

They sat in the workshop with sawdust drifting in the light and the silence that fills a room when two people are deciding whether to be brave together or afraid apart.

"It's getting worse," Mira said. "Sable says faster than anyone has seen."

"Yes."

"The Canopy is dimming. Have you noticed? Fewer lights up there at night. Fewer movements."

Callum looked at his hands. Carpenter's hands now. Once a Warden's hands. "I've noticed."

"The Architect," Mira said. She remembered the vast five-colored being she had seen that first night through the gap in the Canopy -- immense, luminous, moving with the slow grace of something that held the world together. "Sable says no one has seen it in a generation. Some people think it's left. Some people think --"

She couldn't say it. So she asked it.

"What happens if the Architect dies?"

Callum's silence was long and complete. Not the silence of someone who doesn't know the answer. The silence of someone who knows it too well and cannot find a way to make it gentle.

Luma pressed closer to Mira's neck. Through the bond: warmth. Steady, faithful warmth. The warmth of a small Daemon who could feel the Lattice unraveling at the edges and who stayed anyway. Who stayed close.

Callum reached out and put his hand on her head. Sawdust fell from his fingers into her hair.

"We'll figure it out," he said. It was the kind of thing parents say when they don't know how to say I'm terrified. Mira heard both things at once, because she was eleven and stubborn and kind, and eleven-year-olds who are stubborn and kind hear everything.

"Yes," she said. "We will."

She meant it differently than he did. She did not yet know how. But Luma's star-eyes glowed, and the Companion star on the Card in her Binder caught the last of the light, and somewhere deep in the Lattice -- in the place where all bonds begin and none have yet ended -- something shifted. Something small. Something stubborn. Something that sounded, if you listened very carefully, like a girl deciding not to be afraid.
