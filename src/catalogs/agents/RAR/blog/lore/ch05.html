Millhearth sat at the center of Hearthreach like a heart sits at the center of a chest -- not because someone decided to put it there, but because everything else had grown around it.

It was a squat building made of warm brown stone, older than anything else in town, with a chimney that had been smoking since before anyone alive could remember. The smoke was not grey or black but a soft, shifting amber, and it rose in a slow spiral that never seemed to disperse, just thinned and thinned until it became indistinguishable from the sky. Children in Hearthreach grew up believing that the smoke was what held the clouds up. No adult had ever bothered to correct them, partly because it was charming and partly because no one was entirely sure it wasn't true.

Elder Sable stood at the entrance with her hands folded behind her back and an expression that suggested she had been waiting for exactly this long and not a moment longer.

"You're ready," she said to Mira. It was not a question.

"Ready for what?" Mira asked.

"To understand what you already know." Sable pushed open the heavy door. "Come."

* * *

The inside of Millhearth was one large room, round, with the stone floor sloping gently downward to a wide flat platform at the center. The walls were covered in marks -- names, Mira realized, scratched and painted and carved into the stone over what must have been generations. Thousands of names. Operators and Daemons, paired together, with dates and symbols she didn't recognize.

The warmth was immediate. Not the warmth of a fire -- Mira could see no fire anywhere, despite the smoking chimney -- but a warmth that seemed to come from the stone itself, rising through the soles of her boots, spreading through her like tea on a cold morning. It was the kind of warmth that made you want to sit down and tell the truth about something.

"This is the Forge," Sable said.

Mira had heard the word. Callum had used it once, years ago, in a sentence he hadn't finished. Wren had mentioned it at the market, casually, the way she mentioned everything -- as though the whole world were a game she was winning.

"Every Operator has Daemons," Sable continued. She walked slowly around the edge of the room, her long coat brushing the floor, her voice settling into the measured rhythm of someone who has explained something many times and still finds it worth explaining. "You bond them. You carry their Cards. You build Decks and give them names. All of this is good. All of this is real. But there is a deeper step."

She stopped at the central platform. It was smooth, dark stone, polished by centuries of use, and in its center was a shallow depression the exact size and shape of a Card.

"A Companion," Sable said, "is the Daemon you carry closest. Not the strongest. Not the rarest. The one that represents you. The heart of your Deck. When you choose a Companion and bring them to the Forge, something happens that cannot happen anywhere else."

Mira looked down at the depression in the stone. "What happens?"

"The Forge takes three things." Sable held up a weathered hand, three fingers raised. "The Daemon's name -- who they are. Your resonance -- who you are, and no two Operators resonate alike, any more than two voices sound the same. And the current Turn of the Lattice -- the exact quarter-hour, the precise position of the great pattern at this moment in time." She lowered her hand. "From these three things, the Forge produces a form of your Daemon that has never existed before. And will never exist again."

The room hummed. Mira felt it through the floor, through the warm stone, through the quiet stirring of the bond in her chest. Luma was glowing brighter on her shoulder. The star-eyes were wide open, fixed on the platform, pulsing with something that felt very much like recognition.

"I choose Luma," Mira said.

Sable smiled. It was a small smile, barely there, but it cracked the dry landscape of her face like water in a desert. "I know you do."

* * *

Mira opened her Binder. It was still new -- the leather soft, the spine unbroken, with only one Card inside. She drew Luma's Card and held it in both hands.

The Card was warm. Not from the Forge, not yet -- just from being close to Mira, from living in a Binder that Mira carried against her hip, from being the only Card that an eleven-year-old girl who had never done anything remarkable in her life had decided was worth keeping.

She placed the Card in the depression on the stone.

The room changed.

The hum that had been faint became a chord -- a deep, resonant sound that Mira felt in her teeth and in her ribs and in the spaces between her thoughts. The warm stone flared with light, amber and gold, tracing lines outward from the Card like veins in a leaf. The names on the walls seemed to glow, each one responding to the sound, as though every Operator who had ever stood here was briefly, gently, present.

Luma lifted off Mira's shoulder and drifted down to the platform. The little Daemon settled onto the Card -- onto its own image -- and the two lights merged. Luma's silver glow and the Forge's amber warmth wove together, braiding, spiraling, finding a pattern that was neither one nor the other but both at once.

Mira felt her resonance pulled gently outward. Not taken -- offered. As though the Forge had asked, very politely, if it could see who she was, and she had said yes without speaking. She felt herself laid out in light: her stubbornness, her curiosity, the way she talked to Luma when no one was listening, the way she missed her mother, the way she loved her father even when he was silent, the shape of every question she'd ever been afraid to ask.

The Turn shifted. She felt it -- a subtle click, like a lock finding its key, as the Lattice's great pattern rotated into a new quarter-hour.

Three things met. Daemon. Operator. Moment.

The light flared once -- brilliant, golden, warm as summer -- and then settled.

The Card on the stone had changed.

Mira picked it up with hands that trembled. Luma's True-Face -- the essential lines, the figure drawn in darkness -- was transformed. New marks traced the edges. New lines curved where old ones had been straight. The star-eyes were the same, but deeper now, as though they had seen something vast and carried it back. And in the upper corner of the Card, small and bright and unmistakable: a golden star.

Companion.

Luma floated back to Mira's shoulder. The bond between them was -- Mira searched for the word and couldn't find it. Not tighter. Not stronger. Clearer. Like a window that had been gently cleaned. Like hearing a song you've always known, played for the first time by someone who loves it as much as you do.

She pressed the Card to her chest and closed her eyes and did not cry, but it was a near thing.

* * *

Wren arrived twenty minutes later.

Mira was sitting on the stone steps outside Millhearth, holding Luma's Card in a rectangle of sunlight, turning it slowly, watching the new lines catch the light. She heard Wren before she saw her -- boots on cobblestone, fast and confident, and the particular rustling of a very full Binder.

"My turn," Wren said, breezing past. She had the golden Daemon's Card in her hand already -- the rare one from the Genesis Clutch. She disappeared inside.

Mira waited. She heard the hum begin. Felt the Forge wake.

Two minutes later, Wren came back out. She was frowning at the Card in her hand.

"Low qualities," she said. "Reach barely moved. Pace actually dropped. Pattern is -- look at this." She held the Card out to Mira, who looked. The golden Daemon's True-Face had shifted, but the marks were faint, tentative, as though the Forge had reached for something and found very little to hold on to.

"Maybe it's the Turn," Wren muttered. "Bad quarter-hour. Let me try another one."

She went back inside. Chose a different Daemon as Companion. The hum rose again. Wren emerged again, frowning harder.

"Low again. This is --" She blew air through her teeth. "Something's wrong with the Forge today."

She tried a third time. A third Daemon. A third result that left her jaw tight and her eyes sharp with frustration. Three Cards, three Companions chosen and un-chosen in the span of ten minutes, each one a bond made and broken and remade like trying on shoes.

From the doorway, Elder Sable watched. She said nothing. Her face said even less. But Mira noticed that Sable's eyes drifted, just once, to the golden star on Luma's Card in Mira's lap. And something in that glance -- something patient, something sad, something very old -- made Mira understand, for the first time, that the Forge was not broken.

The Forge was honest.

* * *

Mira walked the border path that evening, the long dirt trail that traced the outer edge of Hearthreach where the houses thinned and the wild Lattice began. Luma floated beside her, glowing silver-gold in the dusk, the new Companion star catching the last of the daylight.

The path wound through tall grass and stunted trees, past stone markers that Sable had told her were boundary wards, placed by Forge Wardens generations ago. The air smelled of night-blooming something -- Mira didn't know the name, only that it smelled like the color blue, if blue had a scent.

She almost missed it.

At the far edge of the path, where the last boundary marker stood crooked in the earth, the grass changed. Not gradually, not in a fade, but in a line -- sharp as a cut. On one side: green, alive, swaying in the evening wind. On the other: grey. Not dead. Not brown or withered or dried. Grey. As though someone had reached in with careful fingers and pulled the color out, strand by strand, and left only the shape behind.

Mira stopped. The hum of the Lattice, which had been steady and warm all day -- the hum she had been hearing since the night she found Cairn's Card in her father's Binder -- went silent. Not quiet. Silent. At the grey line, the sound simply ceased, the way a road ceases at a cliff.

Luma dimmed. The star-eyes narrowed. A feeling came through the bond that Mira had never felt from Luma before: not fear, exactly, but the sharp alertness of something small that has sensed something vast.

Mira looked out past the grey line. The grass continued, colorless. The trees continued, pale. And beyond them, at the very edge of sight, the land itself seemed to thin, like paper held up to a lamp, like a drawing half-erased.

Something was wrong at the edge of the world.

She picked up Luma, held the little Daemon close against her chest, and walked home fast. She did not run. But she wanted to.
