Mira Solenne could not sleep. She lay on her back in the narrow bed beneath the eaves, quilt pulled to her chin, eyes wide open, counting the intervals between pulses of the hum.

It had always been there. As long as she could remember, a low, steady note lived beneath everything -- beneath the creak of floorboards, beneath the wind off the Hearthreach hills, beneath her own breathing. Other people did not hear it. She had asked. Her father had given her a strange look when she was seven and said, What hum? Her teacher had told her it was probably the water pipes. The baker's boy had laughed and stuck his tongue out.

So she had stopped asking. She kept the hum to herself, the way you keep a coin in your pocket -- touching it now and then to make sure it was still there.

But tonight the hum was different. Tonight it was louder than it had ever been. Not unpleasant -- never unpleasant -- but insistent, the way a cat presses its head against your hand when it wants to be noticed. The intervals between pulses were getting shorter. The note was climbing.

Mira counted. Eight seconds. Seven. Six.

She sat up.

* * *

The house was small and solid, built from river stone the color of bread crust. Amber lamplight pooled in the kitchen where a single flame still burned in its glass chimney -- her father always left one lit, a habit from his years at the Forge. The air smelled of cedar shavings. Callum Solenne was a carpenter now, and the smell of his work had soaked into every wall, every curtain, every pillowcase in the house. Mira loved that smell. It meant home. It meant her father's big, quiet hands shaping something useful out of raw wood.

She padded down the stairs in bare feet, avoiding the third step that groaned. The kitchen was warm. Embers still glowed in the hearth. On the table, a half-finished chair leg lay beside a mug of cold tea and a whittling knife.

And on the mantelpiece, above the hearth, sat her father's Binder.

She had known it her whole life. A small book, no larger than a journal, bound in leather the deep brown of walnut shells. Brass clasps held it shut. The leather was worn soft at the edges, as though it had been carried in a coat pocket for years and years before coming to rest on this shelf. Mira had never seen her father open it. She had never been allowed to touch it.

It's private, he had told her once, gently, when she reached for it at age five. It belongs to someone I used to be.

She had not understood that then. She was not sure she understood it now.

The hum pulsed. Four seconds. Three.

Mira's hand was on the Binder before she knew she had moved. The leather was warm -- not from the hearth, but from somewhere inside, as if the book had a slow heartbeat of its own. The brass clasps opened with a soft click that sounded, to Mira's ears, like a held breath finally released.

She opened it.

* * *

Most of the pages were blank. Not empty-blank, but waiting-blank -- the kind of blankness that feels like a room with the lights off, where you know there is furniture but cannot see it yet. The pages were thick, cream-colored, with a faint grain like woven cloth. They smelled of something she could not name -- old light, maybe. Warm stone after rain.

She turned pages carefully. Blank. Blank. Blank.

Then -- not blank.

One page held a Card. It was pressed into the paper as though it had grown there, the way lichen grows on a boulder -- slowly, over years, until you cannot tell where the stone ends and the living thing begins. The Card's edges were soft with age. Its colors had faded to the muted tones of an old tapestry.

Three images occupied the Card, stacked like windows in a tower. The first showed a compact, solid figure -- broad-shouldered, patient, the color of deep earth. The second blazed with rich amber and copper, veins of light running through it like roots through soil, magnificent and alive. The third was the simplest: just lines, honest and bare, like a drawing scratched into stone by firelight. Angular. Strong. Marks like deep roots running down from the shoulders into the ground.

Below the images, a name was etched in small, careful letters:

CAIRN

Mira traced the lines of the True-Face with her fingertip. The ink was cool. The hum in her chest was not.

Two seconds. One.

The hum spiked.

* * *

Through the kitchen window, the Canopy ignited.

Mira had seen the Canopy every night of her life -- that vast, shimmering ceiling of light high above the world, so far up it looked like the inside of a shell made of stars. Most nights it rippled gently, the way the surface of a pond moves when a fish turns deep below. Most nights it was beautiful and distant and easy to ignore, the way you ignore the moon because it is always there.

Tonight it flared. The entire sky blazed white, then blue, then a darkness so deep it swallowed the stars, then red like the heart of a forge, then green like the first leaf of spring -- all five colors burning at once, layered on top of each other, impossible and beautiful, a chord struck on an instrument too vast to see.

And behind the light, something moved.

Mira pressed her face to the window. Her breath fogged the glass and she wiped it with her sleeve, frantic, because she could not miss this, she could not look away, not from this --

A silhouette. Vast. Larger than the hills, larger than the whole of Hearthreach, larger than anything she had ever imagined a living thing could be. It moved behind the Canopy the way a whale moves beneath the surface of the sea -- slow, immense, serene. Five colors trailed behind it like the tail of a comet. White and blue and black and red and green, braided together, burning.

It passed overhead in a single heartbeat.

And then it was gone.

The Canopy dimmed. The kitchen was dark again except for the single amber flame. The hum in Mira's chest faded to its usual low note, steady and quiet, as though nothing had happened.

But the Binder in her hands was warm. And the Card called CAIRN seemed to glow faintly at its edges, like an ember that remembers being part of a fire.

Dishes rattled on the shelf. A cup tipped over and rolled in a slow circle. Somewhere upstairs, a door opened.

Footsteps. Heavy, hurried. Her father came down the stairs two at a time, his hair wild, his face tight with something she had never seen there before. He stopped in the kitchen doorway. His eyes found her. Then they found the Binder in her hands.

His face went white.

A long silence filled the kitchen. The amber flame flickered. Outside, the Canopy rippled once, gently, and was still.

Callum Solenne crossed the room slowly. He pulled out a chair and sat down at the kitchen table, across from his daughter, who stood barefoot on the cold stone floor holding his past in her small hands. He looked at the Binder. He looked at the Card. He looked at Mira.

"You heard it. The hum."

She nodded.

He closed his eyes. When he opened them again, something had shifted -- some wall behind his gaze had crumbled, or perhaps had simply been set aside, the way you set aside a tool you have been gripping too tightly for too long.

He sat down heavily.

"Then it's time I told you what this world really is."

Mira sat. The Binder rested between them on the table, open to the page that held CAIRN. The amber flame threw their shadows long across the kitchen wall -- a tall man and a small girl, leaning toward each other, with something ancient and luminous lying between them.

Outside, the Canopy shimmered. And far, far above, in the spaces between the light, something vast and five-colored moved on, carrying its mystery to the other side of the world.
