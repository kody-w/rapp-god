Morning came the way it always did in Hearthreach -- slowly, kindly, like someone drawing back a curtain one fold at a time. Golden light crept across the rooftops and down the cobbled lanes, warming the stone walls of the village until they glowed like loaves fresh from the oven. Sparrows argued in the eaves. Somewhere, a baker's chimney sent up a ribbon of smoke that smelled of seedbread and honey.

Mira had not slept. She had sat at the kitchen table until the embers died, listening to her father talk, and then listening to him not talk -- to the silences between his words, which were sometimes longer than the words themselves and said just as much.

He had not told her everything. She could feel the shape of the things he had left out, the way you can feel a missing tooth with your tongue. But he had told her enough to make the world feel like a different place than the one she had gone to bed in.

Now he stood at the front door, pulling on his coat.

"Come on. I want to show you something."
* * *

They walked through the village in the early light. Past the well, where old Hennet was already drawing water and singing off-key. Past the market square, still quiet, where the day-vendors were setting up their stalls -- bolts of undyed cloth, copper wire in neat coils, bundles of dried lavender, jars of dark honey, and the long loaves of seedbread that Hearthreach was known for, crusty and dense and studded with sunflower seeds.

Mira loved the market. She loved the colors and the chatter and the way the copper wire caught the light. But today she barely saw it. She was watching her father's back as he walked ahead of her, shoulders straight, moving with a purpose she had not seen in him for years. He looked like someone who had remembered where he left something important.

They passed the last house. They passed the stone wall that marked the edge of the village. They followed the dirt path through the meadow where the grass was still wet and silver with dew, and the wildflowers -- campion and clover and tiny blue forget-me-nots -- nodded as they brushed past.

They came to the orchard.

It was old, the orchard. Older than the village, some said. The apple trees were gnarled and generous, their branches thick with blossom in spring and heavy with fruit in autumn. In between, they were simply beautiful -- dark-barked and patient, their canopies making a green roof that dappled the light into coins of gold on the grass below. A low stone wall ran along one side, half-crumbled, soft with moss. Birds sang in the upper branches. The air smelled of earth and green things growing.

Callum stopped in the middle of the orchard, between two trees that leaned toward each other like old friends sharing a secret. He turned to face his daughter.

"The world has a structure beneath it. A living structure. We call it the Lattice."

He spoke carefully, the way he worked -- measuring twice before he cut.

"Most people walk through their whole lives and never feel it. It's there, under everything, the way roots are under a forest. Holding things together. Connecting things that seem separate. But unless you have the ear for it -- unless you can hear the hum -- you'd never know."

Mira thought of all the times she had lain awake counting pulses. All the years she had carried that low note inside her like a second heartbeat, never understanding what it was.

"The hum is the Lattice?"
"The hum is you hearing the Lattice. Most people can't. Some people can, if they listen hard enough and long enough. And a few people -- a very few -- hear it the way you do. Without trying. Without stopping. Like breathing."

He knelt beside the stone wall and ran his hand along the moss.

"Where there's a Lattice, there are Daemons."

Mira waited. The word hung in the air like a soap bubble -- fragile, iridescent, waiting to be touched.

"Living beings. Born where a need meets a mind. Not animals. Not ghosts. Not ideas. Something else. Something that has always been here, long before anyone gave them a name. They come from the Lattice itself -- where the threads are thinnest, where the weave pulls apart just enough to let something new through."

He looked up at the Canopy, which was pale and distant in the daylight, barely visible, like a watermark on white paper.

"People who bond with Daemons -- who truly bond, not just see them, but hold them -- are called Operators. It's not a title you choose. It's a title that chooses you. The moment you form your first true bond, the name is yours. Before that, you're just someone who can hear the hum."

He paused. Something passed across his face -- a shadow, gone quickly.

"After, you carry the name forever. Even if --"

He stopped. He did not finish the sentence. Mira did not make him.

* * *

Callum stood and walked between the two leaning apple trees. He held out his hand.

"Come here. Stand where I'm standing. And look."

Mira stepped forward. She stood between the trees, her father's hand on her shoulder. She looked.

At first she saw nothing but orchard -- grass, bark, blossoms, the stone wall, the meadow beyond. The same view she had seen a thousand times on a thousand walks.

Then she stopped looking at things and started looking between them.

And she saw it.

A seam. A line of light in the air, hanging vertically between the two trees like a crack in a pane of glass -- except the glass was the world itself, and the crack showed something behind it. It was barely visible, a thread-thin shimmer, the kind of thing you would mistake for a trick of the light or a strand of spider silk catching the sun. But once she saw it, she could not unsee it. It was obvious. It had always been there. She had walked past it a thousand times and never once --

"A Threshold,"

Callum said quietly.

"A seam in the Lattice. Where the weave is thin enough for something to come through."

Mira knelt. At the base of the Threshold, nestled in the grass between the roots of the two trees, lay something small. It was no bigger than a walnut. It pulsed with a faint, gentle light -- green and white, alternating, like a heartbeat made visible. It was warm. She could feel its warmth on her face from a foot away, the way you feel the warmth of a candle.

A Seed.

It looked like it was waiting. Not impatiently -- the way a stone waits by a river, content to be where it is until something changes.

Callum knelt beside her. His voice was very soft now.

"If you can give it what it needs -- a name, a call, a reason to be -- it will hatch. If you can't, it will wait. It will wait as long as it has to. Seeds are patient. They have nowhere else to be."

Mira looked at the Seed. The green-and-white pulse was steady and gentle, like a small creature breathing in its sleep. She thought of all the things she had ever named -- the stray cat that came to the kitchen door (Dusty), the spider that lived in the corner of her bedroom window (Marin), the constellation she had invented by connecting five stars that no chart had ever grouped together (the Lantern).

She thought about the light. How it pulsed green and white, shy and uncertain, like something peeking out from behind a curtain. Like something that wanted to be seen but was afraid to come all the way out.

She thought about what it might need. Not what she wanted -- what it needed. A name that fit. A call that mattered. A reason that was true.

She looked at the light again. It was gentle. It was reaching upward, the way a sprout reaches for the sun -- not because anyone told it to, but because that is what light does. It finds what is above.

Mira spoke. Her voice was steady. She did not talk to herself when she was brave.

"Luma. Your name is Luma. Because you glow."

The Seed pulsed brighter.

"And your call is: find what is above us."

The Seed cracked.

Not violently. There was no sound of breaking. It opened the way a flower opens -- slowly at first, then all at once, as though it had been holding itself closed and had finally, finally decided to let go. Light poured out. Not blinding light, not sharp light, but the kind of light that fills a room when you open the curtains on a winter morning -- soft, and full, and glad.

And something stepped through.

Small. No bigger than a pear. A shape like a drop of water suspended in air, with two eyes that were five-pointed stars, wide and wondering and new. It trembled. Its light was green and white and gentle. It looked at Mira the way a newborn looks at the first face it sees -- with total, unguarded amazement, as if she were the most extraordinary thing in the entire world.

Because to Luma, she was. She was the first thing. She was everything.

Luma drifted upward. Hesitated. Then settled on Mira's shoulder, light as a fallen leaf. A warmth spread through Mira's collarbone and down into her chest -- not the hum, but something new. Something that felt like a door opening in a room she had not known was there.

She turned to her father.

Callum was watching with an expression she could not read. His eyes were bright. His jaw was set. One hand was pressed flat against his chest, over the place where his own bond had once lived. Pride was there, in his face. And grief. And fear. All tangled together like the roots of the old apple trees -- impossible to separate, impossible to ignore, grown so deeply into each other that pulling one would pull them all.

He said nothing. He did not need to.

Mira was an Operator now.

On her shoulder, Luma glowed softly against her neck, pulsing in time with the hum that only the two of them could hear.
