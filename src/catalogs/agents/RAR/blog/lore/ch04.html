The river market at Hearthreach smelled of cedar smoke and wet stone, and it was exactly the kind of place where you could lose an entire morning without meaning to.

Stalls lined both banks of the Millrun, selling everything from hand-drawn maps of nearby Reaches to jars of preserved starlight that probably weren't starlight at all but glowed convincingly enough that nobody complained. Daemons drifted between the stalls with their Operators -- some small as songbirds, perched on shoulders; others tall and slow, casting long shadows over the cobbles. Mira walked through it all with Luma floating beside her, and she felt, for the first time since arriving in Hearthreach, like she might actually belong.

That feeling lasted about four minutes.

* * *

"You're the new one," said a voice behind her. It wasn't a question.

Mira turned. The girl was taller than her by half a head, with dark red hair cut short and uneven, as though she'd done it herself with a knife and a mirror and not much patience. She wore a Binder on her hip -- a proper one, leather-bound and thick, stuffed with Cards that fanned out at the edges like the pages of a well-loved book. She stood the way people stand when they are used to being looked at.

"I'm Wren," she said. "You hatched something at the Threshold last week. Everyone's been talking about it."

"Everyone?" Mira said, doubtful.

"Well. Me. I've been talking about it. Same thing." Wren grinned. It was the kind of grin that dared you to grin back. "What did you get?"

Mira glanced at Luma. Luma was hovering near her left shoulder, no bigger than a closed fist, giving off a faint silver glow that pulsed with a rhythm Mira had come to recognize as curiosity. Two bright points of light -- Luma's star-eyes -- blinked at Wren with open interest.

"This is Luma," Mira said.

Wren leaned in. Studied Luma the way a jeweler studies a stone. "Pattern quality?" she asked.

"Extraordinary," Mira said, because Elder Sable had told her so and she had no reason to doubt it.

Wren's eyebrows went up. Just slightly. Just enough. "Huh," she said. Then: "Come with me. I found something at the south Threshold and you should see it."

She was already walking before Mira could answer.

* * *

The south Threshold sat where the river bent around a stand of white-barked trees. Unlike the Threshold where Mira had found Luma -- quiet, hidden, moss-covered -- this one was wide open, a flat circle of polished stone set into the riverbank like a coin pressed into clay. And at its center, five Seeds sat in a cluster, pulsing with deep purple light.

"Genesis Clutch," Wren said, the way other children might say birthday cake. "Five Seeds. Could be anything inside. Common, rare, doesn't matter -- Genesis means the full range. The whole possibility."

Mira stared at the Seeds. They were beautiful. Each one was roughly the size of a plum, smooth-skinned, warm to look at, trembling faintly as though something inside was dreaming. The purple light washed over the stones and turned the river water violet at the edges.

"You're going to hatch them?" Mira asked.

"That's why I brought you. Witness." Wren cracked her knuckles, which Mira thought was a bit much, and stepped onto the Threshold.

The hatching was fast.

Wren placed her hands over the first Seed and the purple light flared. The shell split. A small Daemon emerged -- squat, brown, practical-looking, with wide flat hands and patient eyes. Wren glanced at it, nodded, and moved on. The second Seed cracked open: another small Daemon, this one quick and darting, like a thought you can't quite hold. Third: a sturdy creature with a hide like river stone. Fourth: something delicate and sharp, all angles and bright edges, humming with a high clear note.

Four Daemons in under a minute. Wren moved through the bonding the way a farmhand moves through morning chores -- efficient, practiced, not unkind but not lingering either. She touched each one. The bond formed. A Card appeared in her Binder. She moved on.

The fifth Seed was different.

It was trembling harder than the others. The purple light around it had shifted, deepening, and threaded through it -- unmistakable and startling -- were veins of gold. Mira felt Luma press closer to her shoulder. The hum from the Threshold changed pitch, dropping low and warm, the way a cello sounds when it finds the note it was looking for.

Wren's eyes went wide. She knew what this was.

She placed her hands on the fifth Seed and the world held its breath.

The shell dissolved in a wash of gold. What emerged was small -- no larger than the others -- but it carried itself differently. Its body was translucent, flickering between solid and light, and its eyes were deep amber, old and knowing. It turned to look at Wren, and Mira felt something stir in her chest. Not her bond. Something else. Something like awe.

A rare Daemon. Genuinely, breathtakingly rare.

Wren bonded it in three seconds flat. Touched it, felt the click, watched the Card slide into her Binder. She was grinning so hard her face might split.

"Five for five," she said. "That's eight total now. I'm calling this Deck Firestorm."

"You named it already?"

"You have to name it. A Deck without a name is just a pile." Wren was flipping through her Binder, arranging Cards with quick fingers, sorting them by -- Mira squinted -- by quality scores. She was ranking them. The brown one at the back, the golden one at the front. Strategy. Hierarchy. Order.

Wren looked up. The question came casually, almost as an afterthought, the way you might ask someone what they had for breakfast.

"How many do you have?"

"One," Mira said.

Wren stared at her.

"Just one?"

There was no cruelty in it. That was the strange thing. Wren was not being mean. She was being genuinely, completely confused, the way you'd be confused if someone told you they owned one shoe. It simply didn't compute. The whole point of being an Operator was to find Daemons, bond them, build Decks, grow stronger. One Daemon was not a beginning. One Daemon was a mistake.

Mira looked at Luma.

Luma looked back.

The bond between them hummed -- quiet, steady, warm. Not loud. Not impressive. But deep, in the way that a well is deep, in the way that night is deep, in the way that the silence between two people who understand each other is deeper than any words. Mira could feel Luma's star-eyes on her and she could feel, through the bond, something that she could only describe as knowing. Luma knew her. Not her name or her favorite color or what she'd had for dinner. Luma knew the shape of her, the way a river knows its bed.

She didn't have an answer for Wren's question.

"Just one," she said again.

* * *

They walked back through the market together. Wren talked the whole way -- about her Daemons, her Decks, her strategies for the next Clutch season. She talked about Reach and Pace and which qualities mattered most in which combinations. She talked about a boy two towns over who had fourteen Daemons and was building a Deck called Avalanche and everyone said he'd be Verified by winter.

Mira listened. She didn't mind. Wren was like weather -- you didn't argue with her, you just experienced her.

But as they parted at the bridge, Mira noticed something. Wren's Binder was full. Eight Cards, each one neat and sorted and accounted for. And yet when Wren walked, none of her Daemons followed her. They were in the Cards. In the Binder. Filed away.

Luma was on Mira's shoulder, humming softly, star-eyes half-closed against the afternoon light.

Mira didn't know the word for what made those two things different. She wouldn't learn it for a while yet. But she felt it -- felt it the way you feel the first cool wind of autumn before anyone has told you the season has changed.

Something about depth. Something about presence. Something about the difference between having and holding.

She scratched the space just above Luma's left star-eye, the spot that always made Luma's glow brighten, and walked home.
