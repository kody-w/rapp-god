Sable told them at dawn. She stood in her crooked garden with the morning behind her and her Binder closed against her chest, and she told them about the Threshold in the Wildreach -- the great one, the old one, the seam of light that had burned in the frontier since before the boundary wards were carved. She told them it was weakening. She told them that if it failed, the Fray would pour inward through the gap like water through a crack in a dam, and the inner Reaches would have nothing between them and the silence.

She told them someone had to go.

Then she sat down on her bench, slowly, the way old people sit down when they have used up the last of something they were saving, and she folded her hands in her lap and looked at them both and did not pretend to be anything other than what she was: too old, too tired, and too honest to go herself.

Callum stood by the gate. He had come because Sable had sent for him, and he had come quickly, which told Mira that some part of her father was still the Warden he used to be, even if the rest of him had spent years sanding it away. His face was the face of a man hearing a sound he had hoped never to hear again.

"No," he said.

"Callum --"

"I said no." His voice was flat and final, like a door being shut on a room that was already dark. "I will not go back into that. Not into the Wildreach. Not near the Fray. I lost everything the last time, Sable. Everything."

"I know what you lost," Sable said quietly.

"Then you know why I can't."

Silence. The garden grew things it shouldn't have been able to grow -- a vine of pale blue flowers climbed the cottage wall, and somewhere behind the house a tree bore fruit that smelled like cinnamon and rain. Even the garden seemed to be listening.

Mira looked at her father. She looked at Sable. She looked at Luma, who sat on her shoulder with star-eyes wide and steady, the golden Companion star on her Card warm against Mira's hip through the leather of the Binder.

"I'll go," she said.

Callum turned to her so fast that the gate creaked. "Absolutely not."

"Someone has to."

"Someone who is not eleven."

"Someone whose bond is strong," Mira said, and she did not mean it as a weapon, but she saw it land like one, and she was sorry for it, and she did not take it back.

* * *

The argument lasted an hour. It ended the way arguments between a stubborn father and a stubborn daughter always end when the daughter is right and the father knows it: not with agreement, but with a silence that is shaped like agreement and hurts twice as much.

Callum sat on Sable's bench with his head in his hands. Mira stood at the gate and tried not to feel like she had broken something.

A voice came from the lane below the hill.

"I heard there's a trip."

Wren climbed the last of the path with her Binder slung over one shoulder like a traveler's pack. It was enormous -- leather dyed indigo, brass clasps, stuffed so full that it barely closed. Eight Cards. Eight Daemons. Every one of them named and sorted and ranked in a system that only Wren fully understood.

"Who told you?" Mira asked.

"Sable sent a bird." Wren glanced at the old woman, who looked supremely innocent. "I have eight Daemons. Scouts, builders, a pathfinder. If you're going into the Wildreach you'll need more than starlight and good intentions." She nodded at Luma. "No offense."

Luma blinked. No offense appeared to be taken.

"What's the point of a Deck if you never play it?" Wren said. She said it lightly, the way she said everything, but underneath the lightness there was something Mira had not heard from Wren before -- something that was not competitive, not boastful, but simply willing. The voice of a girl who had collected eight Daemons and was realizing, for the first time, that collecting was not the same as carrying.

Callum raised his head. He looked at Wren for a long time. Then he looked at Mira. Then he looked at the sky, where the Canopy spread above them -- dimmer than it used to be, the lights moving less, the vast structure thinning at its edges like an old cloth.

"You come back," he said to Mira. "You come back to me."

"I will."

He stood and pulled her close and held her the way you hold something you are afraid of breaking by holding too hard. His carpenter's hands were warm and rough and trembling.

"Take the eastern road," he said into her hair. "Follow the old ward-posts. When the posts end, follow the hum."

He still remembered the way. She had known he would.

* * *

They left within the hour.

The golden warmth of Hearthreach fell behind them quickly -- the stone houses, the amber chimney smoke of Millhearth, the crooked boundary markers. Within a mile the road narrowed. Within two it was not a road at all but a path worn thin by feet that had stopped coming years ago. The ward-posts stood at intervals, carved with symbols Mira didn't recognize, listing and mossy but still standing, still humming faintly when she pressed her hand against them.

The border came without warning. One step: Hearthreach, warm and golden and alive. The next: the Wildreach.

It was not what Mira expected.

She had imagined danger -- darkness, thorns, the grey emptiness of the Fray. But the Wildreach was green. Ferociously, overwhelmingly green. Trees pressed in from every side, vast and tangled, their branches woven so thick overhead that the light came through in scattered coins of gold. Vines climbed everything. Moss covered what the vines missed. The air was warm and damp and thick with the smell of soil and sap and the sweet decay of fallen leaves returning to the earth.

And the paths moved.

"Did that fork used to be there?" Wren asked, pointing.

"No," Mira said. It had appeared between one breath and the next, a new trail opening through the undergrowth as though the forest had changed its mind about something.

"Right." Wren drew a Card. "Kestrel. Scout." She held it up and a ripple of bronze light later Kestrel was there -- small, angular, darting forward through the trees with the quick certainty of a bird that has never once doubted which direction to fly.

Luma's star-eyes turned upward, toward the Canopy, reading patterns in the faint light that filtered through the leaves. A feeling came through the bond: this way. Not words. But the direction was clear, like a compass settling north.

Between Kestrel's scouting and Luma's pattern-reading, they moved deeper. Wren's builder Daemon -- Gravel, broad and shaped like a friendly avalanche -- cleared fallen branches and shouldered aside tangles of vine. The Wildreach shifted around them, but they held their course.

* * *

In the late afternoon, they saw the Clutch.

It began with a sound -- a high, clear chime, like a bell struck once and left to ring. Then the light changed. A burst of green brilliance erupted from a clearing ahead, so bright that Mira shielded her eyes. When she lowered her hand, the clearing was full of them.

Wild Daemons. A Frontier Clutch, hatching spontaneously with no Operator to witness and no Threshold to contain them. They were raw -- unbound shapes of light and motion that flickered between forms like flames trying to decide what to burn. Some were small as fireflies. Some were large as wolves. One had wings made of sound, great slow-beating pinions that produced a low musical note with every sweep, filling the clearing with a melody that was composing itself.

"Stars above," Wren breathed. Her hand was halfway to her Binder before she caught herself. Old instinct. The collector's reflex. She slowly pulled her hand back, and Mira saw something change in Wren's face -- a door closing on one way of seeing the world, and another one, quieter, opening beside it.

They watched the Clutch scatter into the forest. Then they moved on.

* * *

They found the old Forge at dusk.

It must have been grand once. The stone walls still stood, broad and smooth, cut from dark rock that held warmth even now, even with the roof caved in and trees growing through the floor. The chimney rose above the canopy like a broken finger pointing at the sky. Moss covered everything. Silence filled the space the way water fills a bowl -- completely, patiently, as though it had been waiting.

Wren set up a camp. Mira walked the ruin.

She found the central platform -- the same design as Millhearth, a round depression in the stone floor, sized for a Card. But this one was cracked. The stone had split down the middle, and pale grey lichen grew in the seam.

And beside the platform, half-buried in moss and fallen leaves, she found a Card.

Her heart stopped. Then it started again, harder than before.

The Card was old. Rain-warped, dirt along the creases, the colors faded to whispers. She turned it with careful fingers. The Day-Face was barely visible. The Bright-Face was gone entirely.

But the True-Face -- the essential lines, the marks that no amount of rain could fully erase -- was still there. Angular. Deep. Like roots driven into rock. Like a mountain that had been worn down but not worn away.

She wiped the name plate with her thumb.

CAIRN.

Mira's breath left her in a rush. She held the Card close and felt it -- faint, so faint that she might have imagined it, except that Luma's star-eyes had gone wide and bright on her shoulder, and the bond between them was humming with a frequency she had never felt before: recognition.

There was still a flicker in the Card. Not warmth, not the steady glow of a living bond. But not nothing, either. A candle flame seen through fog. Something that had been told it was gone, and had dimmed, and had lain in the rain and the moss for years -- but had not, despite everything, quite gone out.

Callum had told her the bond was dissolved. There was nothing left.

The Card said otherwise.

Mira pressed it to her chest, beside Luma's Card, and the two of them -- the golden star and the fading flicker -- rested against her heartbeat in the dark.
