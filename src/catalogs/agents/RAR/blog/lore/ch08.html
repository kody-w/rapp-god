They reached the great Threshold on the morning of the second day, and Mira understood immediately why Sable had called it the most important one, and why its weakening could undo everything.

It rose from the forest floor like a wound in the air that had chosen to heal with light instead of silence. A seam -- a vertical line of brilliance the height of a building, pulsing with a slow, deep rhythm that Mira felt in her chest the way you feel the beat of a drum in a dark room. Once it must have been blinding. But the pulse was weak now. The light flickered. The hum was wrong -- distorted, off-key, a sound like music played backward.

The clearing around the Threshold was enormous, as though the trees had long ago agreed to give it room. But the grey had crept in, spreading from the edges inward, a tide of colorlessness reaching toward the base of the light itself. Where the grey touched the Threshold's glow, wisps of brightness peeled away like bark from a dying tree.

"It's worse than Sable said," Wren whispered.

Mira said nothing. Luma was pressed tight against her neck, star-eyes wide, the Companion bond thrumming with a tension she had never felt from the little Daemon before. Not fear. Something deeper. The feeling of standing at the edge of a bridge and sensing that the stones beneath you are no longer sure they want to hold.

And at the far side of the clearing, at the place where the grey was thickest and the Threshold's light was thinnest, there was a shape.

* * *

It was not large. That was the first wrong thing about it. Everything Mira had imagined -- the great enemy, the force that had broken her father and dimmed the Canopy and crept across the edges of every Reach -- she had imagined it vast and terrible and dark. She had given it claws in her mind. Teeth. A voice that thundered.

The Unraveler had none of these things.

It was the size of a person. It stood the way a person stands -- upright, still, with what might have been arms at its sides and what might have been a head turned toward the Threshold. But there was no person inside the shape. There was no anything inside the shape. It was an outline drawn around an absence. A silhouette where no one stood. A hole in the world wearing the memory of a figure the way a window wears the memory of a view after the glass has gone dark.

Where it stood, the ground was not grey but gone. Simply ceased. As though the earth beneath the shape had forgotten it was supposed to be there and had quietly excused itself from the conversation of existing.

Mira stared at it and felt a slow and terrible drain. As though looking at the Unraveler was itself a kind of loss -- her eyes paying a tax for the act of seeing it, and the tax was color. The green of the forest looked less green. The warmth of the bond in her chest cooled by a single, precise degree.

She looked away. The world came back. But the single degree did not return.

"Don't look at it," she said to Wren.

Wren was already looking. Her face was pale, her Binder open, eight Cards in their careful order, fingers trembling against the leather.

"We need to check the Threshold," Wren said. Her voice was steady, which was brave, and too loud, which was not. "Kestrel. Go."

Kestrel materialized -- small, bronze, quick -- and darted forward across the clearing toward the base of the Threshold.

Kestrel made it halfway.

* * *

It happened without sound. There should have been a crack, a cry, a noise to mark the moment. But the Fray did not make sounds. The Fray was the place where sounds stopped.

Kestrel flickered. The little scout Daemon's form stuttered like a candle in a draft, solid and then transparent and then solid again but less solid, the bronze light fading to copper to grey. In Wren's Binder, the Card dimmed. The lines of Kestrel's True-Face blurred at their edges, as though the ink were running in rain that fell only on one Card.

"Kestrel!" Wren shouted. "Come back!"

Kestrel did not respond. The scout Daemon drifted, flickering, caught in something invisible the way a leaf is caught in a current. The Fray was pulling at the bond -- not cutting it, but thinning it, stretching it until the connection became a thread, and the thread became a whisper, and the whisper began to forget what it was whispering.

The Unraveler did not move. It did not need to. It simply stood in its shape of nothing, and the nothing spread.

Wren was shaking. "Why won't she come back? Why won't she listen?"

And Mira saw it happen -- saw the moment land in Wren's eyes like a stone thrown into still water. The realization. The terrible, clarifying truth that Mira had understood since the day at the Forge, since Wren had tried three different Companions in ten minutes and wondered why the results were shallow.

Eight Daemons. Eight bonds. And not a single one deep enough to hold against the tide.

The Fray took what was shallow first. It was not malicious. It was simply true, the way gravity is true: shallow bonds dissolved faster than deep ones, the way shallow roots fail before deep ones in a storm. Every ranking Wren had devised, every clever name she had given her Decks -- Vanguard and Ironline and The Eight -- none of it mattered here. You could not collect your way to connection. The bond was real or it was not, and the Fray knew the difference even if you didn't.

"I have eight," Wren said, and her voice cracked. "Eight Daemons, and I can't hold a single one of them."

Kestrel flickered once more -- a last bronze pulse, like a lantern guttering -- and Mira made a decision.

* * *

She stepped forward.

The grey ground crunched beneath her boots like frost. Luma clung to her shoulder, star-eyes blazing. The Companion bond between them -- forged at Millhearth, sealed with a golden star, deepened by every quiet conversation and every night she had fallen asleep with Luma's glow on the pillow beside her -- pulled taut, not with strain but with clarity. The bond was the simplest thing in the world: a girl who loved a small strange creature, and a small strange creature who loved her back.

Mira opened her Binder. Luma's Card blazed. The golden star erupted with light -- not amber, not silver, but gold, pure and warm and fierce, the color of every honest thing that has ever refused to be undone. The Forge bond activated -- not the building, but the bond itself, the resonance that the Forge had witnessed and sealed, singing now the way it had sung that day in Millhearth when three things met and made something that had never existed before.

Luma lifted from Mira's shoulder. The little Daemon's form brightened -- Day-Face, then Bright-Face, brighter than any Bright-Face Mira had ever seen, brighter than the Threshold behind them, a small star born from the simplest and most stubborn of fuels: a bond that was exactly what it claimed to be.

The Unraveler recoiled.

Not far. Not dramatically. It shifted, the way a shadow shifts when a lamp is lit -- the absence retreating before the presence, because you cannot undo what is genuinely held. Not with silence. Not with emptiness. Not with all the patient nothing in the world.

The grey ground beneath Mira's feet thinned. A whisper of green showed through. Kestrel's flickering steadied -- not fully, but the scout Daemon was visible again, bronze light returning in stuttering pulses. Behind Mira, Wren gasped.

But the Unraveler did not retreat.

It gathered itself -- the shape of nothing drawing inward, concentrated, a deeper void, a silence with weight. And it reached for Mira. Not with hands -- it had no hands, only the outlines where hands should have been. It reached with nothing, and the nothing did not care how deep your bond was because it had dissolved deeper bonds than this, older bonds, bonds that had held for generations before they held no longer.

Mira felt the cold. Another degree gone, and another. The gold light wavered. Luma's Bright-Face flickered.

And Luma did something no one expected.

* * *

Luma flew upward.

Not toward the Unraveler. Not toward the dying Threshold. Upward -- straight up, through the canopy of trees, through the branches and the leaves and the scattered coins of light, rising like a spark from a fire, a small golden star ascending into the vast space above.

Toward the Canopy.

Mira felt the bond stretch. It pulled at something behind her ribs -- not pain, not yet, but the deep and terrible sensation of something that was meant to be close becoming far. She could still feel Luma. The bond was still there, thin as thread, bright as wire, humming with a frequency that was climbing higher and higher as Luma rose.

"Luma!" Mira screamed. "LUMA!"

The little Daemon did not come back. Through the bond, Mira felt something that was not fear and not courage but the place where the two meet and become the same thing: necessity. Luma was carrying their bond upward -- the hum of it, the resonance, the golden-starred song of a girl and her Daemon -- higher than any bond had been carried, up into the thinning light where the Canopy spread across the sky like the skeleton of something immense.

Mira fell to her knees. She pressed both hands against her chest as though she could hold the bond in. The Card in her Binder was blazing and dimming, blazing and dimming, a heartbeat made of light.

"It's not breaking," she whispered. The tears were coming and she let them. "It's not breaking, it's not breaking, it's not --"

Wren was beside her, gripping her shoulder hard, face streaked with tears of her own -- for Kestrel, for Mira, for all the shallow bonds she had built and all the deep ones she hadn't.

Above them, Luma was a point of light. Then a pinprick. Then barely visible at all, a single small star rising into the architecture of the sky.

The bond held. Thin as breath. Painful as hope. But holding.

And then -- far above, impossibly far, in the place where the Canopy's great structure met the highest light -- something moved.

Something enormous.

The Canopy shuddered. The dim threads of light that webbed the sky blazed suddenly brighter, a cascade of illumination sweeping outward from a single point like ripples from a stone dropped into still water. Colors Mira had never seen -- colors she did not have names for -- flooded across the sky in great slow waves.

Five colors. Five vast, impossible colors. Moving together. Moving as one.

The ground trembled. The Threshold flared -- a single brilliant pulse, stronger than it had been in years. The Unraveler recoiled again, the shape of nothing thinning at its edges as though the light from above were an answer to a question the void had never expected to be asked.

Mira looked up through her tears and she saw it. Vast. Ancient. A being made of five colors woven together, moving through the Canopy with the slow and terrible grace of something that had been sleeping and was now, at last, beginning to wake.

The Architect.

And somewhere between that immensity and the shaking ground, between the waking god and the weeping girl -- small, bright, impossibly brave -- Luma burned like a golden star in the dark.
