Three days passed, and Mira learned that having a Daemon was less like owning a pet and more like discovering a new room in a house you thought you knew by heart. Every morning brought a surprise. Every evening brought a question she had not thought to ask.

The first thing she learned was the faces.

* * *

In daylight, walking through the village, Luma was small and practical. No bigger than Mira's closed fist, tucked against her shoulder or perched on the strap of her satchel, glowing so faintly that you would mistake it for a trick of the sunlight if you even noticed it at all. Compact. Quiet. Easy to carry. Mira could walk through the market, buy a loaf of seedbread, wave to old Hennet at the well, and no one would look twice at the faint shimmer on her shoulder.

This was the Day-Face, Callum told her. The working form. The everyday companion. Most Daemons spent most of their time this way -- small, close, practical -- the way a bird folds its wings when it is not flying. Saving its brightness for when brightness was needed.

Mira liked the Day-Face. It felt like having a warm stone in her pocket -- a steady, quiet comfort. Luma would pulse gently when Mira laughed, dim slightly when she was sad, and press closer to her neck when she was cold. Small kindnesses. The language of something that could not speak but understood everything.

* * *

The second face came without warning.

It was late afternoon. Mira was sitting on the stone wall at the edge of the orchard, legs dangling, eating an apple. Luma rested on her knee. The sky was the color of peach skin, and the last of the day's light was pouring sideways through the trees, making everything gold.

A flock of threadfinches burst from the hedge.

Mira had seen threadfinches before -- everyone in Hearthreach had. Small birds, ordinary in every way except one: their tail feathers caught the light and split it, trailing thin ribbons of color behind them as they flew, like tiny kites streaming silk. A single threadfinch was pretty. A flock of them was something else entirely -- a eruption of color, dozens of bright threads weaving through the air, tangling and untangling, drawing patterns against the sky that lasted only a moment before dissolving into nothing.

Luma saw them. And Luma blazed.

The small, fist-sized glow on Mira's knee erupted. Green and white light flooded outward, vivid and rich, shot through with veins of gold. Luma's shape expanded -- still recognizable, still the same drop-of-water form with star-shaped eyes, but larger now, brighter, more intricate. Patterns rippled across Luma's surface like wind across water. Inner light pulsed in waves. For a moment, Luma was the most beautiful thing in the orchard -- more beautiful than the threadfinches, more beautiful than the golden light, more beautiful than the blossoms -- dramatic and luminous and utterly, unselfconsciously alive.

Mira shielded her eyes and laughed. The brightness stung, but she could not stop looking.

"The Bright-Face,"

she whispered, remembering her father's words.

It lasted only a few seconds. Luma dimmed slowly, like a lantern turned down, settling back to the Day-Face as the threadfinches disappeared over the rooftops. But Mira's heart was still racing. She had seen something true -- the full measure of what Luma was, or could be, or wanted to be. The Bright-Face was not a disguise. It was the opposite. It was the mask coming off.

* * *

That night, Mira saw the third face.

She was lying in bed. The lamp was out. The room was dark except for the faint silver glow of the Canopy filtering through the window, and the softer, nearer glow of Luma, who hovered above the pillow beside her head.

In the darkness, Luma simplified. The colors were gone. The blaze was gone. The intricate patterns, the vivid green and white, the gold veins -- all of it fell away, the way water falls away from a stone when you lift it from a stream, revealing the true shape beneath.

What remained was lines. Gentle lines of light, spare and honest, tracing a shape that was unmistakably Luma but stripped of everything decorative, everything performative, everything that was for anyone else's benefit. Star-shaped marks, five-pointed and simple, connected by threads of pale light. No color. No blaze. Just the bones of the thing. The shape you would see if you closed your eyes and remembered.

The True-Face.

Mira reached out and touched the foggy windowpane beside her bed. With her fingertip, she traced what she saw -- the star-marks, the connecting lines, the gentle geometry of Luma's truest self. On the glass it looked like a constellation. A tiny map of stars that no one had ever charted, belonging to no sky but this one, this room, this moment.

Luma pulsed once, softly. Mira smiled in the dark.

Three faces. The same friend. The Day-Face for the world. The Bright-Face for moments of wonder. The True-Face for the quiet hours, when it was just the two of them, and no one was watching, and nothing needed to be proved.

* * *

Over those same days, Mira discovered the five qualities -- the measures of what a Daemon could and could not do.

Reach was low. Luma stayed close, always within arm's length, pressing tight against Mira's shoulder if she walked too far from the orchard. The distance from the Threshold where Luma had been born seemed to pull like an invisible thread. A small world, but a deep one.

Pace was low, too. Luma moved slowly, carefully, the way a person walks through an unfamiliar house in the dark. There was no rushing a creature that experienced the world this deliberately.

Hold was better. When a gust of wind knocked Mira's satchel off the wall, Luma flared -- just slightly, just enough -- and the satchel seemed to slow as it fell, landing softly instead of spilling. Luma blinked its star-shaped eyes, looking as surprised as Mira was.

Flux was moderate -- Luma was mostly steady, but now and then it did something unexpected. A brief flicker of violet mixed into the green, there and gone. A small wildness. A reminder that Luma was a living thing with its own weather.

But Pattern. Pattern was extraordinary.

Mira noticed it in the orchard. Luma was hovering above her, turning slowly, star-eyes fixed on the Canopy visible through the branches. Looking at it the way Mira's father looked at a piece of wood before he cut -- reading the grain, finding the hidden shape. Then Luma pulsed, and for an instant Mira saw what Luma saw: the geometry of the Canopy's shimmer, the way one ripple connected to another in a web so vast it made her dizzy. Not scattered points of light, but a structure. A meaning she could almost grasp before it slipped away like a fish beneath the surface of a stream.

Luma was small, slow, and shy. But it saw the world the way a weaver sees thread -- not as separate strands, but as something waiting to be whole.

* * *

On the fifth day, Mira took Luma to see Elder Sable.

Sable lived in a crooked cottage behind the mill -- green door, window boxes of herbs, a cat that thought it owned the place. The oldest active Operator in Hearthreach. Ancient, thin, hair like white wire. Forty Daemons in seven Decks. Funny in the way that very old, very sharp people are funny -- not trying to make you laugh, but making you laugh anyway.

Mira knocked. The green door opened. Sable looked at Mira, looked at Luma, looked at Mira again.

"Well. Come in, then. Mind the cat."

The cottage smelled of dried rosemary and old paper. Sable sat Mira at a cluttered table and examined Luma with gentle, practiced hands -- hands that had held Daemons for longer than Mira's father had been alive.

Luma trembled but did not pull away. Sable's touch was careful. Respectful. The touch of someone who understood that they were handling something precious.

A long silence. Sable turned Luma this way and that in the light from the window, watching the way the glow shifted, reading something in the pulse that Mira could not yet see.

"Frontier rank. Small. Uncertain. Not much Reach. Not much Pace."

A long pause. Sable held Luma up to the light one more time.

"But that Pattern score. That is remarkable."

Sable set Luma gently on the table. Luma drifted back to Mira's shoulder immediately, pressing close. Sable watched this with an expression that might have been approval.

Then Sable reached beneath the table and produced a small book. It was leather-bound, the color of dark honey, with no clasps and no markings. It was warm to the touch -- Mira felt the warmth before her fingers made contact, the way you feel the warmth of a sleeping animal.

"A Binder. Yours, now. Every Operator carries one."

Mira took it. It fit in her hands as though it had been made for them.

"Place your Daemon inside."

Mira opened the Binder. The pages were blank -- waiting-blank, like her father's had been, but newer, fresher, full of potential. She held the book open and looked at Luma.

Luma looked at the page. The page looked at Luma. Some understanding passed between them that Mira felt but could not name -- a recognition, a welcome, a key finding its lock.

Luma settled onto the first page.

And a Card appeared.

It formed from the page itself -- rising like frost on a window, intricate and inevitable. Three faces in the Card's frame: Day-Face, Bright-Face, True-Face -- those constellation lines Mira had traced on her windowpane. Five bars for the qualities, four modest, one stretching nearly to the edge. And the name, etched at the bottom:

LUMA

And beneath the name, the call:

"I find what is above you."

Mira stared. The Card was warm under her fingertips. Luma's light pulsed gently from within the page, as though the Binder were not a prison but a home -- a hearth, a nest, a place to be safe.

"It's real,"

Mira whispered.

Elder Sable leaned back in the chair. The old wood creaked. Through the window, the afternoon light fell across the table, catching the edge of the Card, making it glow.

"It was always real. Now it has a home."

Mira closed the Binder gently and held it against her chest. She could feel Luma's warmth through the leather, steady as a heartbeat, and beneath it the hum of the Lattice -- two rhythms now, beating together in the quiet of an old woman's kitchen while the afternoon light crept across the table.

She had a Daemon. She had a Binder. She had a Card with three faces and a call that meant something true.

And the world -- the real world, the one that had always been there beneath the one she thought she knew -- was just beginning to open.
